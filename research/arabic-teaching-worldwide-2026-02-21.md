# Arabic Language Teaching Worldwide: A Comprehensive Research Report

*February 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

Arabic is spoken by over 400 million people and holds official status in 25 countries, yet the teaching of Arabic -- both as a first language in the Arab world and as a foreign/heritage language elsewhere -- faces a set of deeply entrenched structural problems. The 2021 World Bank report "Advancing Arabic Language Teaching and Learning" identified that **more than half of children in MENA countries experience "learning poverty"** -- they cannot read and understand an age-appropriate text by age 10. In lower- and middle-income Arab countries, this figure rises to **63 percent**. These outcomes persist even in wealthy Gulf states, where spending on education is substantial.

This report examines Arabic language instruction across four broad contexts: native-language education in Arab countries, Arabic education in non-Arab Muslim-majority countries, foreign-language and heritage-language programs in Western countries, and the cross-cutting methodological debates that connect them all.

## 1. Arab Countries Where Arabic Is L1

### 1.1 General Curricular Framework

Across the Arab world, education systems teach Modern Standard Arabic (MSA, or *al-fusha*) as the medium of instruction from primary school onward. MSA is the language of textbooks, exams, official documents, and most printed matter. However, no child grows up speaking MSA at home -- all children arrive at school speaking a regional dialect (*ammiyya* / *darija*) that differs from MSA in phonology, morphology, syntax, and vocabulary to varying degrees.

This creates a situation that the World Bank (2021) describes as follows: "Children are taught to read and write in a rigid way with a focus on rules, grammar, and accuracy. Children also experience a lack of playfulness and inquiry that are needed to fully engage them in literacy learning."

### 1.2 Egypt

Egypt's Arabic curriculum has been widely criticized. The gap between Egyptian colloquial Arabic (ECA) and MSA is significant, with **only approximately 40% vocabulary overlap** between Egyptian textbooks and the Egyptian dialect (Al-Fanar Media, 2022). Children in public schools who only speak Arabic at home are known to have difficulty reading and writing -- MSA functions as a partially foreign register.

Key problems include:
- Vocabulary introduced in textbooks does not match words children recently learned or use at home
- Heavy emphasis on rote memorization, where vocabulary retention ends at the exam
- Curriculum lacks connection to students' lived experiences
- Pedagogical best practice -- starting with words close to both MSA and dialect -- is rarely followed

Egypt's PIRLS 2021 reading score of approximately **378** (on a 500-point centerpoint scale) places it among the lowest-performing participating countries worldwide. The World Bank found that **69% of Egyptian children** are below minimum reading proficiency.

### 1.3 Saudi Arabia

Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 has driven extensive education reform, but much of the focus has been on English-language instruction and STEM subjects rather than Arabic pedagogy per se. Arabic instruction hours have actually been *reduced* to make room for English from the first grade (previously starting at grade 4) and new subjects like critical thinking.

Saudi Arabia scored approximately **449** on PIRLS 2021, well below the international average of 500 but above several other Arab countries. The OECD's 2020 review of Saudi education identified fragmented teacher preparation and content-heavy assessment systems as ongoing challenges.

The Khbrat program sends Saudi teachers abroad for professional development, but these partnerships focus primarily on English-speaking university programs. A national curriculum framework emphasizing skills and competencies has been developed but is still being rolled out.

### 1.4 United Arab Emirates

The UAE presents a paradox: it is one of the wealthiest Arab countries and invests heavily in education, yet faces a crisis in Arabic-language proficiency among its own citizens. **Over 70% of Emirati children** now enroll in English-medium instruction (EMI) schools, perceived as offering superior educational outcomes. The result is a generational linguistic shift: students are becoming more competent in English and less fluent in Arabic.

Despite UAE Vision 2021's goal for 90% Arabic literacy, **95% of Emirati students entering universities require remedial Arabic courses** -- a staggering gap between policy aspiration and educational reality. The UAE scored approximately **483** on PIRLS 2021 (among higher-performing Arab states, but still below the 500 international average).

In response, the UAE has increased Arabic instruction time in kindergartens, mandated frameworks for Arabic/Islamic education in private schools, and extended project-based learning to all Cycle 2 students. In 2025, AI was introduced as a compulsory subject from kindergarten through Grade 12. These reforms address the structural problem but do not directly tackle Arabic pedagogy.

### 1.5 Jordan

Jordan has a learning poverty rate **exceeding 60%**, with over half of 10-year-olds unable to read and comprehend age-appropriate Arabic texts. Jordan scored approximately **381** on PIRLS 2021.

Jordan stands out for having launched a **National Arabic Literacy Strategy 2023-2028**, developed by the Ministry of Education with support from the Queen Rania Foundation (QRF). The strategy explicitly aims to shift from content-driven curriculum to competency-based instruction focused on literacy and numeracy.

However, implementation faces structural barriers: fragmented teacher training, overcrowded classrooms, content-heavy assessment systems, and resource constraints. The QRF's "Let's Read Fluently!" intervention, grounded in cognitive science and adapted for the Jordanian context, has shown preliminary positive impact on oral reading fluency, syllable identification, and word decoding.

### 1.6 Lebanon

Lebanon's education system is distinctive for its trilingual policy, adopted in 1997: Arabic, French, and English are all taught from early years, with English or French serving as mandatory media of instruction for mathematics and sciences.

This creates a complex linguistic environment:
- Children speak Lebanese Arabic (a dialect) at home
- They learn MSA as a "high" register in school
- They learn most technical subjects in French or English

Researchers voice concern that this multilingual arrangement may produce students "who may not have any real proficiency levels in any of the languages" and that Arabic is losing ground compared to the foreign languages. Code-switching between Lebanese, MSA, French, and English is pervasive in both public and private sectors.

### 1.7 Qatar

Qatar launched its "Education for a New Era" reform in 2001-2004, one of the most ambitious in the region. The reform established a system of Independent Schools, developed national curriculum standards in four core subjects (Arabic, English, mathematics, science), and invested heavily in teacher professional development.

Qatar scored approximately **485** on PIRLS 2021. While this is among the higher Arab-country scores, it remains below the international average of 500 and far behind top performers like Singapore (587) or Ireland (577).

## 2. Diglossia: The Foundational Challenge

### 2.1 The Nature of the Problem

Arabic diglossia -- the coexistence of MSA ("high" variety) and spoken dialects ("low" variety) within the same speech community -- is arguably the single most important factor in Arabic education outcomes worldwide.

Key research findings (summarized from Al-Fanar Media, 2022; Frontiers in Education, 2025; World Bank, 2021):

- **In five wealthy Gulf states** (Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE), **36.6%** of students cannot grasp simple text by fourth grade -- compared to **2.7%** in economically comparable non-Arab nations
- **Palestinian children** possess just **21.2% shared vocabulary** with MSA
- Diglossia negatively impacts "phonological awareness, morphological awareness, phonological representation, word decoding, reading fluency, and letter naming"
- The negative impact is **disproportionately greater for families of lower socioeconomic status**
- International assessments (PISA 2018, PIRLS 2016 and 2021) consistently show Arab children scoring below average, even in wealthy countries

### 2.2 The Maghreb: An Extreme Case

The Maghreb countries (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia) present the most extreme form of Arabic diglossia, compounded by multilingualism.

**Morocco** scored approximately **372** on PIRLS 2021 -- one of the lowest scores globally. The World Bank found that **67% of grade 2 children in Morocco** could not answer a single reading comprehension question correctly. Moroccan Darija diverges dramatically from MSA, incorporating substantial French, Berber, and Spanish loanwords. The country's Arabization policy, launched after independence in 1956, replaced French with Arabic in public education, but:
- Many math and science teachers of the transitional generation were educated in French and struggle to teach in MSA
- Wealthy families bypass the system through private schools (often French-medium), exacerbating socioeconomic inequality
- French continues to dominate at secondary and postsecondary levels in technical fields
- After 30+ years of Arabization, language education challenges "have yet to be fully addressed"

**Algeria** faced an even more dramatic post-independence transition. In 1962, approximately 18,000 of 23,500 teachers (60%) and 1,400 of 2,000 professors (70%) left the country. The gap was filled by staff from Arab, European, and Asian countries "who often did not meet the educational levels required." By the 1980s, Arabization was largely complete in government and education, but quality concerns persist.

**Tunisia**, once praised for its education system, has faced declining outcomes. The diglossia challenge is compounded by the historical role of French in technical and scientific education.

### 2.3 Resistance to Addressing Diglossia

Many Arab policymakers resist acknowledging diglossia as a factor in education outcomes. Some frame dialect-inclusive education as threatening Arab nationalism or Islamic values, since MSA is closely associated with the Quran. Researchers argue that dialects should serve "as a steppingstone to reach al-Fusha" rather than being treated as corruptions to be eliminated -- but this remains politically charged.

## 3. Non-Arab Muslim-Majority Countries

### 3.1 Overview

Approximately **85% of the world's 1.8+ billion Muslims live in non-Arab countries**. In these countries, Arabic instruction is primarily motivated by religious literacy -- the ability to read the Quran, understand Islamic jurisprudence, and access classical religious texts. Communicative competence in spoken Arabic is rarely a goal.

### 3.2 Turkey

Turkey's Imam Hatip schools form the largest institutional framework for Arabic instruction outside the Arab world. These vocational high schools allocate approximately **25% of curriculum time** to religious courses including Quran, Arabic, biography of the Prophet, hadith, jurisprudence, and Quranic interpretation.

Arabic is taught alongside Turkish as a working language for religious study. However, the focus is on reading comprehension of classical texts rather than communicative ability. Imam Hatip enrollment has expanded significantly under the AKP government, but the Arabic taught remains largely textual and grammatical. Mainstream Turkish schools offer two hours of compulsory religious education per week but minimal Arabic content.

### 3.3 Iran

In Iran's *hawza* (seminary) system, Arabic occupies a central place. The Qom Seminary curriculum includes Arabic grammar (*sarf* and *nahw*), rhetoric, and classical literature as prerequisites for advanced study of jurisprudence, theology, and Quranic sciences.

The hawza curriculum takes students from foundational studies to advanced mastery, with rigorous training in classical Arabic throughout. However, this is elite religious education -- the general Iranian school system teaches Arabic as a subject but does not aim for communicative proficiency. Arabic study in mainstream Iranian schools focuses on Quranic vocabulary and basic grammar, not spoken fluency.

### 3.4 Pakistan

Pakistan's madrasa system, largely following the Nizami curriculum adopted by Deoband seminary in 1867, teaches Arabic as a component of approximately twenty subjects divided into *al-uloom an-naqliya* (transmitted sciences: Quran, hadith, jurisprudence) and *al-uloom al-aqliya* (rational sciences: grammar, rhetoric, logic, philosophy).

In Deobandi schools, Urdu, Arabic, and Persian are taught; up to 8th grade, government textbooks are used; after that, traditional Arabic and Persian texts replace them. The curriculum is described as "very old and inflexible" and focused on grammatical analysis of classical texts rather than communicative skills. Few Pakistani madrasa graduates can conduct a conversation in Arabic despite years of study.

### 3.5 Indonesia

Indonesia has the world's largest Muslim population and a substantial Arabic education infrastructure:

- **Pesantren** (traditional Islamic boarding schools): Approximately 27,000 pesantren serve millions of students. They prioritize Quranic memorization and study of classical Islamic texts. The grammar-translation method and rote memorization remain the prevailing instructional models, "often at the expense of communicative competence."
- **National curriculum**: Indonesia has institutionalized Arabic education by integrating it into national curricula under the Ministry of Religious Affairs. Madrasah schools offer Arabic alongside secular subjects.
- **Modern pesantren**: Some modern pesantren (notably Gontor) have adopted more communicative approaches, blending grammar-translation with communicative language teaching. However, research shows Indonesian language interference remains a significant barrier.

Indonesia is developing a **Competency-Based Arabic Curriculum** aligned with the *Merdeka Belajar* ("Freedom to Learn") framework, though implementation remains uneven.

### 3.6 Malaysia

Malaysia's **j-QAF program** (Jawi, Quran, Arabic, Fard Ayn), introduced in 2005, represents one of the most systematic state-level efforts to teach Arabic in a non-Arab country. Four teaching models were developed: Jawi Remedial Class, Khatam Quran, Arabic Language Communication, and Smart Prayer Camp.

Arabic is taught in both primary and secondary schools as part of the national curriculum, with specialized religious secondary schools offering more intensive Arabic instruction. However, survey data shows teachers report being dissatisfied with lesson time allocation (58.6% dissatisfied), and Arabic proficiency outcomes remain modest for most students.

## 4. Western Countries

### 4.1 United States

#### University Programs

Arabic study in U.S. higher education grew dramatically after September 11, 2001, but has since declined. Key data from the **MLA Language Enrollment Survey**:
- Arabic enrollments **declined by over 27%** between 2016 and 2021
- **80 fewer institutions** reported Arabic programs between 2016 and 2021
- Enrollments declined in all three most recent census periods (2013, 2016, 2021)
- This is part of a broader 16.6% decline in all non-English language enrollment

Despite declining enrollment, several elite programs remain influential:

- **Georgetown University**: Home to the oldest undergraduate Arabic major in the U.S. and the Al-Kitaab textbook series (published by Georgetown University Press), which is used in the majority of American university Arabic programs. The first five semesters of study are intensive, emphasizing "fluency, advanced reading comprehension, and the ability to write and interact at advanced levels."

- **University of Maryland Arabic Flagship Program**: Combines intensive domestic study with immersion in Morocco, targeting professional-level (ILR 2+/3) proficiency.

- **Michigan State University Arabic Flagship (ALIF)**: Targets ILR 3/3+ (Superior level), among the most ambitious proficiency goals in U.S. Arabic education.

- **Middlebury Language Schools**: Offers an 8-week summer immersion program, "one of the oldest and most respected Arabic-learning programs in the United States," supplemented by a study-abroad program at the University of Jordan in Amman.

#### The Al-Kitaab Curriculum

The Al-Kitaab series by Kristen Brustad, Mahmoud Al-Batal, and Abbas Al-Tonsi dominates American university Arabic instruction. It was unusual when first published for integrating both MSA and Egyptian Arabic; the current third edition includes MSA, Egyptian, and Levantine Arabic. This integrated approach -- teaching dialect alongside MSA -- was controversial but has become increasingly accepted, with research showing "equally robust outcomes as in a traditional, MSA-only curriculum."

#### K-12 Programs

Arabic in American K-12 education remains rare:
- As of 2017, approximately **3,740 students** were enrolled in Arabic classes across **161 high schools** in 38 states, with approximately 108 teachers
- Arabic represents only **2.5%** of Less Commonly Taught Languages enrollment
- The first Arabic dual immersion program opened in **Houston in 2015** (Arabic Immersion Magnet School, now serving 611 students PK-8)
- A handful of programs exist in New York, California, Arizona, and Minnesota, some funded by the Qatar Foundation
- Michigan State's K-12 curriculum project trains Arabic teachers in over 17 states

However, Arabic is the **second-most spoken home language** for English-language learners in K-12 public schools (after Spanish), with approximately **110,000 students** reporting Arabic as their home language.

#### Heritage Speaker Programs

Arabic heritage language education has a long history in the U.S. through community-based schools organized by Arab Americans to teach their children language and cultural heritage. Institutional programs include:
- **Brooklyn College** (CUNY): Heritage Language Program for Arabic speakers with family/community ties but limited formal education in the language
- **Bellevue School District** (Washington state): Launched an Arabic Bilingual Program in 2023-2024, one of very few public school dual-language programs in Arabic
- **STARTALK**: A federal program that funds summer Arabic camps and teacher training

The **Coalition of Community-Based Language Schools** supports heritage language schools nationwide, but resources and quality vary enormously.

### 4.2 United Kingdom

**SOAS (School of Oriental and African Studies)** at the University of London is the premier Arabic studies institution in the UK, ranked 7th nationally for African and Middle Eastern Studies and 2nd for graduate prospects. The BA Arabic program trains students in "both written and spoken forms of the language" with cultural and historical context. SOAS teaches Literary Arabic alongside Maghrebian Arabic (Tunisian, Algerian, Moroccan), Oriental Arabic, and Syrian-Lebanese-Palestinian Arabic.

The UK also has a substantial Arabic-speaking diaspora, particularly from Iraq, Yemen, and North Africa, but heritage language programs are less institutionalized than in the U.S.

### 4.3 France

France has the largest Arabic-speaking diaspora in Europe (primarily Maghrebi origin). **INALCO** (Institut National des Langues et Civilisations Orientales) in Paris is the leading institution, teaching Literary Arabic and multiple dialect varieties (Tunisian, Algerian, Moroccan, Syrian-Lebanese-Palestinian). INALCO prepares students for the national *agregation* and *CAPES* competitive examinations in Arabic, qualifying them to teach in secondary schools.

Arabic was historically offered in French secondary schools but has been declining. INALCO has advocated for "the return of Arabic in secondary schools," recognizing the gap between the large Arabic-speaking population and institutional language instruction.

### 4.4 Germany

Germany's *Herkunftssprachenunterricht* (Heritage Language Education, HSU) system, particularly in North Rhine-Westphalia, offers mother-tongue instruction for children of immigrant backgrounds, including Arabic. This program originated in the 1950s-1970s guest worker era as preparation for children's "return" to home countries -- now in its third and fourth generation, the program has shifted toward supporting multilingual competence.

University-level Arabic is available at institutions like the Free University of Berlin and Hamburg, though Germany lacks a single dominant national Arabic studies center comparable to SOAS or Georgetown.

## 5. Teaching Methodology Comparison

### 5.1 Grammar-Translation (Traditional)

The **grammar-translation method** remains dominant across most of the Arabic-teaching world:
- Arab countries (native speakers): Focus on grammatical rules, parsing (*i'rab*), and classical text analysis
- Pesantren/madrasa systems: Focus on classical Arabic grammar (*nahw* and *sarf*) for reading religious texts
- Traditional university programs: Systematic study of morphological patterns and grammatical paradigms

**Strengths**: Builds strong grammatical foundations; develops reading comprehension of classical texts; preserves connection to literary and religious heritage.

**Weaknesses**: "Frequently fail[s] to equip learners with the skills needed for dynamic communication in authentic settings, restricting the learners' ability to interact fluently with native speakers or navigate the sociocultural nuances of Arabic-speaking environments."

### 5.2 Communicative Language Teaching (CLT)

The **communicative approach** has gained ground primarily in Western university programs and some modern Arabic-as-a-foreign-language centers:
- Georgetown/Al-Kitaab curriculum: Integrated approach combining MSA and dialect
- Middlebury immersion: Total immersion with the Language Pledge
- Qasid Arabic Institute (Amman): Intensive communicative instruction

Research consistently shows learners **prefer communicative methods** and are "dissatisfied with teaching methodology emphasizing memorization." Studies from multiple contexts find communicative approaches produce better oral proficiency and real-world communication skills.

### 5.3 Integrated and Hybrid Approaches

The emerging consensus favors **integrated models** that combine elements of grammar-translation, communicative methods, and technology-based approaches. Key developments:

- **Dialect integration**: Programs at Georgetown, Penn, and Bahrain are teaching dialects alongside MSA, treating code-switching as a linguistic strength
- **Root and pattern analysis**: Morphological elements "serve as a bridge between al-Fusha and dialect vocabulary acquisition"
- **Phonological awareness**: Teaching how sounds vary across dialects enables transfer learning between Arabic varieties
- **Technology**: Digital tools for vocabulary building, spaced repetition, and media-based listening comprehension

### 5.4 The Science of Reading and Arabic

A critical gap exists between reading science research and Arabic instruction practice. The World Bank (2021) found that "findings from research into the science of language learning have yet to translate into Arabic instructional practices, and **systematic phonics instruction is rare in schools of Arabic-speaking countries** despite research showing its importance."

Research by Habib Taha and others shows that Arabic-specific phonics instruction should include:
- Phonemic and phonological awareness
- Alphabetic principle (with attention to Arabic-specific challenges like letter connectivity and dot positioning)
- Explicit word study and vocabulary instruction
- Morphological, orthographic, and diglossic awareness
- Graduated diacritics instruction (reading with and without *tashkeel*)

The Queen Rania Foundation's "Let's Read Fluently!" program in Jordan is one of the few large-scale attempts to apply science-of-reading principles to Arabic instruction, with preliminary evidence showing positive impact on reading fluency and word decoding.

## 6. Known Problems and Criticisms

### 6.1 The World Bank's Assessment (2021)

The World Bank's landmark report "Advancing Arabic Language Teaching and Learning: A Path to Reducing Learning Poverty in the Middle East and North Africa" identified a systemic crisis:

**Scale of the problem**:
- More than half of MENA children experience learning poverty
- In middle- and low-income MENA countries, the rate is **63%**
- In six lower-income Arab states (Algeria, Egypt, Jordan, Morocco, Oman, Tunisia), the average below-minimum proficiency rate is **59%**, with Egypt at **69%**
- In Iraq, **52%** of grade 2 children could not answer a single reading comprehension question; in Morocco, **67%**; in Yemen, **81%**

**Root causes identified**:
1. Lower enrollment in early childhood education compared to other regions
2. Children less likely to have children's books at home, school libraries, or be asked to read chapter books
3. Rigid teaching focused on rules, grammar, and accuracy rather than engagement
4. Teachers are "themselves the product of ineffective Arabic language education" and often uncomfortable using MSA
5. Very few teacher preparation programs include targeted Arabic teaching studies based on reading science
6. Parents in MENA read to children at home at rates of **20-25%**, compared to **80%** in countries like Australia, Netherlands, and New Zealand

### 6.2 International Assessment Performance (PIRLS 2021)

PIRLS 2021 scores for participating Arab countries (international centerpoint = 500):

| Country | Score | Relative Position |
|---------|-------|-------------------|
| Qatar | ~485 | Below international average |
| UAE | ~483 | Below international average |
| Bahrain | ~458 | Below international average |
| Saudi Arabia | ~449 | Below international average |
| Oman | ~429 | Well below average |
| Jordan | ~381 | Far below average |
| Egypt | ~378 | Far below average |
| Morocco | ~372 | Among lowest globally |

For comparison, Singapore scored 587, Ireland 577, and Hong Kong 573. The international centerpoint is 500. Every participating Arab country scored below the international average. Even the wealthiest Arab states (Qatar, UAE) scored roughly 20 points below average, demonstrating that the problem is not primarily about funding.

### 6.3 The Children's Literature Deficit

A critical contributing factor is the scarcity of quality Arabic reading materials for children:
- The Arab world (population 362+ million in 2012) produced roughly the same number of books as Romania (population 21 million) or Ukraine (population 46 million)
- Quality children's books are prohibitively priced, especially imported ones
- Award-winning Arabic children's books are often "appreciated more by adults than the target audience"
- Enforcement of copyright is weak, book distribution networks are poor, bookstores and public libraries are scarce
- Only 49 Arabic young adult literature books were published or republished in English between 1988 and 2010

### 6.4 The English Displacement Problem

Across the Arab world, particularly in the Gulf, there is growing concern about English displacing Arabic in education. In the UAE, over 70% of Emirati children attend English-medium schools. Universities in Qatar and UAE have promoted English-medium instruction as part of modernization.

Critics warn that "learning English helps students access global jobs, but it threatens the region's culture." Proponents of English-medium instruction respond that it is necessary for economic competitiveness. The tension remains unresolved, with Arabic often losing ground in both scenarios: traditional Arabic pedagogy produces poor outcomes, while English-medium instruction sidelines Arabic altogether.

### 6.5 Teacher Quality

The World Bank (2021) identifies teacher quality as perhaps the most intractable problem: "Teachers, including many Arabic language teachers, are themselves the product of ineffective Arabic language education and often are not comfortable using [MSA] as a medium of instruction." This creates a vicious cycle -- poorly trained teachers produce students who become poorly trained teachers.

## 7. Notable Reform Efforts

### 7.1 Jordan: Queen Rania Foundation and "Let's Read Fluently!"

The QRF's intervention, developed in partnership with the Education Endowment Foundation (UK) and BHP Foundation, is grounded in cognitive science and represents one of the most evidence-based Arabic literacy programs in the region. The "Let's Read Fluently!" program targets oral reading fluency, syllable identification, and word decoding in early grades. Jordan's National Arabic Literacy Strategy 2023-2028 provides a five-year framework for systemic reform.

### 7.2 UAE: Arab Reading Challenge

Launched by Mohammed bin Rashid Al Maktoum in 2015, the Arab Reading Challenge has grown from **3.6 million student participants** in its first year to **28.2 million participants from 50 countries** in its latest edition, making it arguably the world's largest reading initiative. Students must read and summarize 50 books. The school with the best reading initiatives receives $1 million in funding. A companion "Reading Nation" initiative aims to equip over 3,000 libraries with approximately 5 million books in Arab countries with acute book shortages.

While impressive in scale, the challenge primarily addresses motivation and access rather than the underlying pedagogical problems. It does not directly address diglossia, teacher quality, or reading instruction methodology.

### 7.3 Qatar: Education for a New Era

Qatar's 2001-2004 reform created Independent Schools with new curriculum standards, enhanced teacher development, and international benchmarking. The reform produced some improvements -- students are now in "learner-centered classrooms within improved facilities where better-prepared and better-trained teachers guide them" -- but results remain below international averages.

### 7.4 Integrated Dialect-MSA Instruction (Multiple Institutions)

A growing movement in Western university programs advocates teaching Arabic "as one language" -- integrating dialects alongside MSA. Leading institutions include:
- **University of Pennsylvania**: Provides "cheat sheets showing word equivalencies across dialects"
- **Georgetown University in Qatar**: Students create dialect-specific class dictionaries
- **University of Bahrain**: Students compare advertisements across Arab countries
- **Carnegie Mellon University**: Researches code-switching competency development

Research presented at the 2020 Middle East Studies Association (MESA) conference showed "equally robust outcomes as in a traditional, MSA-only curriculum" -- debunking fears that dialect instruction undermines MSA learning.

### 7.5 Science-of-Reading Adaptations for Arabic

The **Qatar Reading Foundation** (QRF) and researchers including Habib Taha are adapting the science of reading to Arabic's specific orthographic and linguistic characteristics. Key areas of progress:
- Development of systematic Arabic phonics curricula
- Research on diacritics instruction (graduated removal as proficiency increases)
- Phonological awareness training adapted for Arabic diglossia
- Brain imaging research to understand Arabic reading processes

### 7.6 U.S. Flagship Programs

The Arabic Flagship programs at the University of Maryland and Michigan State University represent the most intensive Arabic instruction available in the English-speaking world. Michigan State targets ILR 3/3+ (Superior), equivalent to near-native reading and listening comprehension. These programs demonstrate that high proficiency is achievable but requires multi-year, immersive commitment including overseas study.

### 7.7 Indonesia: Merdeka Belajar Integration

Indonesia's development of a competency-based Arabic curriculum aligned with the *Merdeka Belajar* framework represents an attempt to move pesantren Arabic instruction from pure grammar-translation toward communicative competence. Some modern pesantren, notably Gontor, have successfully blended traditional and communicative methods.

## 8. Conclusions and Key Themes

### 8.1 The Problem Is Not Primarily About Money

Wealthy Gulf states (UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia) spend lavishly on education yet produce reading outcomes below the international average. The 36.6% below-minimum-proficiency rate in five Gulf states compares to 2.7% in economically comparable non-Arab nations. The problem is structural and pedagogical, not financial.

### 8.2 Diglossia Is the Elephant in the Room

The refusal to systematically address the gap between MSA and spoken dialects undermines Arabic instruction at every level. Children arrive at school speaking a language that shares as little as 21% of its vocabulary with what they encounter in textbooks. Until education systems acknowledge this reality and use dialects as bridges to MSA -- rather than treating them as corruptions -- learning outcomes will remain poor.

### 8.3 The Science of Reading Has Barely Reached Arabic

Systematic phonics instruction, shown to be effective across languages, is "rare in schools of Arabic-speaking countries." The gap between reading science research and Arabic classroom practice is decades wide. The Queen Rania Foundation's work in Jordan and the Qatar Reading Foundation's research offer promising but still localized exceptions.

### 8.4 Teacher Quality Is the Binding Constraint

Teachers cannot teach what they were never taught. The World Bank's finding that Arabic language teachers are "themselves the product of ineffective Arabic language education" describes a self-perpetuating cycle that no amount of curriculum reform can overcome without massive investment in teacher preparation.

### 8.5 The Religious-Communicative Divide

In non-Arab Muslim countries, Arabic instruction remains overwhelmingly focused on religious literacy (reading Quran, classical texts) rather than communicative competence. While this serves legitimate religious purposes, it means that the vast majority of the world's Arabic learners never develop the ability to use Arabic for real-world communication.

### 8.6 Western Programs Are Small but Innovative

Western university programs are leading methodological innovation (dialect integration, communicative methods, immersion models) but serve tiny numbers of students and face declining enrollment. The 27% drop in U.S. university Arabic enrollment between 2016-2021 threatens the sustainability of even successful programs.

### 8.7 Heritage Speakers Are an Untapped Resource

With 110,000 Arabic-speaking students in U.S. K-12 schools alone, and millions in European diaspora communities, heritage speakers represent an enormous untapped resource. Yet heritage language programs remain underfunded and fragmented, with only a handful of dual-language programs in the entire United States.
