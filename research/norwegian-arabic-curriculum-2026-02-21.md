# Norwegian High School Arabic Curriculum — Benchmarking

## The Only Textbook Series

Two volumes by **John Erik Sætren** (PhD Arabic, UiB) and **Afrah Mohammed Ghalion** (Arabic teacher, Stavanger katedralskole), published by Fagbokforlaget:

### Volume I: "Lær arabisk!" (Arabisk for vg2)
- **Published**: 2014, 308 pages
- **Structure**: 12 chapters across 4 themes
  - Theme 1 (ch 1-3): The Arabic language — script, dialects vs. MSA, cultural overview
  - Theme 2 (ch 4-6): Self, family & friends — greetings, names, origins, age, family, professions, colors
  - Theme 3 (ch 7-9): Home & school — rooms, directions, school life, transport, days of the week, telling time
  - Theme 4 (ch 10-12): After school — leisure, food/restaurants, shopping/prices
- **Target**: Students with zero prior Arabic knowledge (Nivå I / fremmedspråk nivå I)

### Volume II: "Arabisk for vg3"
- **Published**: 2016, 250 pages
- **Structure**: 5 thematic units
  - Final theme: specialization topics (politics, literature, extracting info from advanced texts)
- **Target**: Students who completed Nivå I (Nivå II / fremmedspråk nivå II)

Both include vocabulary lists per chapter (ordlister), exercises, basic grammar, and companion web resources with audio and interactive exercises.

## Level Achieved

### UDIR official position on Arabic and CEFR

The Norwegian Directorate for Education (UDIR) explicitly distinguishes:
- **European languages** (French, Spanish, German, etc.): Nivå I ≈ CEFR A1, Nivå II ≈ CEFR A2
- **Non-European languages** (Arabic, Japanese, Chinese, Urdu): **CEFR does not apply**. Students "may not reach A1" after completing Nivå I.

This means after **two full years** of Arabic instruction (vg2 + vg3, ~190 teaching hours each at 2-3 hours/week), a Norwegian high school student is realistically at **sub-A1 to A1**. The writing system overhead and morphological complexity eat into what would otherwise be vocabulary and grammar time.

### Why Arabic is harder to benchmark
- Script learning (letter forms, connected writing) consumes early months
- Root-and-pattern morphology is fundamentally different from European inflection
- No cognates with Norwegian (unlike French/Spanish/German)
- Diglossia: MSA taught in school vs. dialects in media

## Vocabulary Size (Estimated)

No published word counts for the textbooks exist. Cross-referenced estimates:

| Source | Estimate |
|--------|----------|
| CEFR A1 Arabic (eArabicLearning) | 500-700 words |
| Typical A1 textbook (12 chapters × ~40 words/chapter) | ~480 words |
| Volume I topics (survival: greetings, family, school, food, shopping) | ~400-600 words |
| Volume II adds 5 thematic units | ~300-500 additional words |
| **Total estimate after both volumes** | **~700-1000 words** |

For reference, CEFR benchmarks for Arabic vocabulary:
- A1: 500-700 words
- A2: 800-1,200 words
- B1: 1,500-2,000 words
- B2: 2,500-3,500 words

## Relevance for Alif

As of 2026-02-21, Alif tracks ~500+ lemmas in active learning (acquiring + FSRS), meaning the app already covers roughly the equivalent of the entire Volume I vocabulary, achieved in weeks rather than a school year.

This benchmark is useful for:
- **Framing progress**: "You know as many words as a Norwegian high school graduate in Arabic" is a motivating milestone
- **Vocabulary selection**: The textbook themes (family, school, food, shopping, leisure) represent the canonical A1 survival vocabulary — useful for ensuring Alif's word introduction covers these domains
- **Setting expectations**: Even formal instruction only reaches sub-A1 to A1 after 2 years — the difficulty of Arabic for Nordic speakers is real
