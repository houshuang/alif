## Key Publications (2024–2025)

### 1. Transculturation in Arabic Literacy Education (Iversen, 2025)
**"Transculturation in Arabic literacy education within and beyond mainstream education in Norway and Sweden"**
European Educational Research Journal. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/14749041241235718)

**Method:** 8 weeks of ethnographic fieldwork in each country — a supplementary heritage language school in Norway and three mainstream schools in Sweden (two Arabic heritage language teachers).

**Findings:**
- Sweden integrates Arabic heritage language education into mainstream schools. This leads Arabic instruction to align closely with official Swedish literacy expectations.
- Norway organizes it as supplementary (weekend) schools. This preserves more traditional Arabic teaching approaches.
- The organizational model shapes how transculturation (cultural transformation across boundaries) plays out in the classroom.

**Relevance to Alif:** The tension between traditional Arabic literacy pedagogy (rote, grammar-translation) and modern communicative approaches mirrors our design choices about how to teach vocabulary and reading. Students in the Norwegian supplementary model were more likely to experience a disconnect between how they learn Arabic and how they learn at school.

### 2. Policies, Conditions and Student Experiences in Arabic and Sámi Heritage Language Education (Iversen, 2025)
**"Policies, conditions and student experiences in Arabic and Sámi heritage language education"**
Scandinavian Journal of Educational Research. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/00313831.2025.2600956)

**Method:** 10 weeks of ethnographic fieldwork comparing Sámi heritage language education (in public schools) and Arabic heritage language education (in a complementary weekend school) in Norway.

**Findings:**
- Both Arabic and Sámi heritage language education are marginalized forms of education.
- Sámi students reported encouragement from peers and pride in their heritage.
- Arabic students reported **keeping their heritage language education secret** from their mainstream school peers.
- Heritage language education organized *within* mainstream schooling fosters more positive student attitudes than when organized *outside* it.

**Relevance to Alif:** The stigma finding is striking — Arabic learners hiding their language study from peers. A private, self-directed app like Alif may actually be an advantage here, removing the social visibility issue entirely. Also relevant to how we think about motivation and identity in the learning experience.

### 3. Translanguaging in Transnational Digital Arabic Heritage Language Education (Amro & Iversen, 2025)
**"Translanguaging in transnational digital Arabic heritage language education"**
European Educational Research Journal. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/14749041241311958)

**Method:** 7 months of digital linguistic ethnography — online classroom observations, interviews with teacher and students, document collection. Studied a heritage language teacher of Arabic conducting classes digitally across borders.

**Findings:**
- The teacher used translanguaging (drawing on students' full multilingual repertoires — Arabic, Norwegian, other languages) as a deliberate pedagogical strategy.
- Digital format enables transnational heritage language education, connecting Arabic-speaking students across countries.
- Translanguaging created a "safe space" and supported literacy development in Modern Standard Arabic.
- Students' diverse linguistic resources were treated as assets, not interference.

**Relevance to Alif:** Validates our approach of showing English translations alongside Arabic (a form of translanguaging scaffolding). Also relevant to the question of whether to include transliteration — research here suggests leveraging all available linguistic resources is productive, not a crutch. The digital/transnational aspect is directly relevant since Alif is a digital tool.

### 4. Pre-service Teachers' Agency to Enact Multilingual Pedagogies (Iversen, 2025)
**"Pre-service teachers' agency to enact multilingual pedagogies: A longitudinal case study"**
European Educational Research Journal. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1177/14749041241290381)

**Method:** Longitudinal case study — interviews with 5 Norwegian pre-service teachers at the start and end of their teacher education.

**Findings:**
- By graduation, teachers had altered beliefs, gained knowledge, and developed more flexible approaches to multilingual learners.
- However, the monolingual structures of Norwegian schools still constrain their practical agency.
- Teachers need both knowledge AND structural conditions to enact multilingual pedagogies.

**Relevance to Alif:** Less directly relevant but confirms that institutional structures shape what's possible in language learning. An independent app can bypass many of these structural constraints.

## Key Themes Relevant to Alif

1. **Organizational model matters:** How heritage language education is structured (mainstream vs. supplementary) fundamentally shapes the learning experience. Alif as a self-directed tool sits outside both models.

2. **Translanguaging is productive:** Using multiple languages in the learning process (L1 English + L2 Arabic) is validated by this research, not seen as a deficit. Our English glosses, transliteration, and root explanations are supported.

3. **Identity and motivation:** Arabic heritage learners face unique identity pressures. Even non-heritage learners of Arabic may benefit from private, low-stakes practice environments.

4. **Digital delivery is viable:** Amro's PhD specifically studies digital Arabic language education and finds it effective for heritage language maintenance.

5. **MSA literacy is the shared goal:** Across diverse Arabic-speaking backgrounds, Modern Standard Arabic literacy is the common target — aligned with Alif's MSA focus.
