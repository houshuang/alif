# Arabic Explained in Arabic — Podcast Format Design

**Date**: 2026-03-25
**Status**: Research complete, ready for prototyping
**Context**: Learner knows ~800 MSA words at high stability. Goal: audio content where Arabic is used to explain Arabic, building from extremely simple to moderately complex.

---

## Part 1: Research Findings

### 1.1 Comprehensible Input (CI) Podcasts — What Works

**Dreaming Spanish** (30M+ downloads) structures content at graded levels (Superbeginner → Beginner → Intermediate → Advanced), each rated 1-100 for difficulty. Key insight: they don't teach grammar explicitly — they surround the learner with understandable content that gets slightly harder over time. The superbeginner level uses extremely short utterances, very slow speech, and heavy use of visual context (which we don't have in audio — so we must compensate with verbal scaffolding).

**Russian With Max** (Comprehensible Russian, 356 episodes, A2-B2) uses slow, clear narration with "repetitions, synonyms, explanations" as the bridge to understanding. Episodes include vocabulary explanations in-context, not as isolated definitions. Each episode stays on a single topic, recycling the same vocabulary in multiple sentence frames.

**Nihongo con Teppei** speaks entirely in Japanese, deliberately dropping to simpler Japanese when introducing a difficult word. He occasionally drops in a single English word only when circumlocution in Japanese would be hopelessly confusing. Episodes are ~20 minutes, daily, covering varied topics with natural but slowed speech.

**InnerFrench** (Hugo Cotton, 30M downloads) targets intermediate learners with 100% French episodes structured like well-researched essays. Each episode is on a compelling topic — the learner forgets they're "learning French" and just follows the content. Key principle: if the topic is genuinely interesting, the learner will push through ambiguity to understand it.

**Common patterns across all successful CI podcasts:**
1. Stay in the target language as much as possible (90-100%)
2. Speak slowly and clearly, with natural pauses
3. Repeat key structures in varied forms (not verbatim repetition)
4. Stay on one topic/theme per episode (narrow listening)
5. Use circumlocution — explain hard words using simpler words the learner already knows
6. Keep episodes short enough for re-listening (5-15 minutes for beginners)

### 1.2 TPRS Circling — The Gold Standard for Oral Repetition

TPRS (Teaching Proficiency through Reading and Storytelling) provides the most rigorous framework for making a single piece of language maximally comprehensible through audio. The core technique is **circling**:

Given a statement like "أحمد يشرب القهوة" (Ahmad drinks coffee):
1. **Yes question**: هل أحمد يشرب القهوة؟ (Does Ahmad drink coffee?) → نعم!
2. **No question**: هل أحمد يشرب الشاي؟ (Does Ahmad drink tea?) → لا!
3. **Either/or**: هل أحمد يشرب القهوة أو الشاي؟ (Coffee or tea?) → القهوة!
4. **Wh-question**: ماذا يشرب أحمد؟ (What does Ahmad drink?) → القهوة!
5. **Restatement**: نعم، أحمد يشرب القهوة. (Yes, Ahmad drinks coffee.)

This produces ~50-100 repetitions of each target structure per story in a classroom. For audio-only, we adapt this: the teacher asks the question, pauses, then answers it — modeling both the question and the expected response.

**Key TPRS principle**: one new structure at a time. All surrounding vocabulary must already be comprehensible. This maps perfectly to our known-word list approach.

### 1.3 Circumlocution — Explaining Arabic in Arabic

Circumlocution is the art of describing a concept using only words the listener already knows. Strategies:

1. **Category + description**: مكان نذهب إليه لنشتري الأشياء (a place we go to buy things → store/سوق)
2. **Synonym chain**: كبير... كبير جداً... ضخم (big... very big... enormous)
3. **Contrast**: ليس بارداً، ليس حاراً... معتدل (not cold, not hot... moderate)
4. **Function/purpose**: نستخدمها للكتابة (we use it for writing → pen/قلم)
5. **Physical description**: حيوان كبير في أفريقيا، لونه رمادي (a big animal in Africa, its color is gray → elephant/فيل)

For our 800-word learner, this works well because at that level you have enough concrete nouns, basic verbs (go, eat, drink, see, hear, want, know, like), adjectives (big, small, good, bad, new, old, hot, cold), and connectors (and, but, because, when, in, from, to) to describe almost anything through composition.

### 1.4 Narrow Listening — Why One Topic Works

Krashen's "narrow listening" approach shows that hearing the same topic discussed multiple times (by different speakers, or in different framings) produces rapid comprehension gains. Research shows:
- 3-4 repetitions extract most value from a passage for intermediate learners
- Beginners benefit from 7-8 repetitions, gaining ~10% more comprehension each pass
- Vocabulary from topic N carries forward to topic N+1 if topics are related

This suggests our episodes should: (a) stay on one theme, (b) be designed for re-listening, and (c) connect to adjacent episodes via shared vocabulary.

### 1.5 Scaffolded Rephrasing — The Core Technique

The most powerful technique for audio-only CI is **saying the same idea 3-4 ways**, each slightly different:

```
الولد يأكل.           (The boy eats.)
الولد يأكل تفاحة.      (The boy eats an apple.)
الولد الصغير يأكل تفاحة حمراء.  (The little boy eats a red apple.)
الولد جائع، فيأكل تفاحة حمراء كبيرة.  (The boy is hungry, so he eats a big red apple.)
```

Each repetition adds one element while keeping the core structure. The listener hears the same skeleton 4 times, but each version adds richness. By the 4th version, they comprehend a sentence that would have been opaque as a standalone.

This is different from verbatim repetition (which gets boring fast). Each rephrase is genuinely new information that happens to reuse the same structures.

## Part 2: Pedagogical Principles

Three principles for the "Arabic Explained in Arabic" format:

### Principle 1: The 90/10 Comprehensibility Rule

At least 90% of the words the listener hears should already be in their known vocabulary. The remaining 10% is the "i+1" — new words that are immediately explained via circumlocution using words from the 90%. This is stricter than typical CI (which allows ~80%) because we are audio-only with no visual support, no gestures, no facial expressions. The listener's only scaffold is the words they already know.

**Practical implication**: Before introducing a new word, the teacher must "prepare the ground" by using 2-3 sentences of pure known vocabulary to establish context. The new word should arrive when the listener is already guessing what it might be.

### Principle 2: Spiral Repetition (Never Verbatim)

Every key word or structure appears at least 5 times in an episode, but never in the exact same sentence. Each repetition changes something:
- Different subject (أحمد → ليلى → أنا)
- Different tense (يذهب → ذهب → سيذهب)
- Different emotional frame (يريد → لا يريد → يحب أن)
- Different complexity (simple → compound → with subordinate clause)

This gives the brain 5 different "angles" on the same word, building a rich representation rather than a single frozen association.

### Principle 3: The Warm Teacher Voice (Emotional Scaffolding)

The tone must feel like a patient, warm teacher who is genuinely talking to you — not reading a script. This means:
- Short rhetorical questions that create a sense of dialogue: هل تعرف؟ (do you know?)
- Validation phrases: ممتاز! أحسنت! (excellent! well done!)
- Thinking-aloud connectors: يعني... (that is to say...) لأنّ... (because...)
- Occasional direct address: الآن... اسمع (now... listen)

These don't teach vocabulary directly but they create a parasocial relationship — the feeling of being in a conversation rather than listening to a recording. This lowers the affective filter (Krashen's term for emotional barrier to acquisition) and keeps the listener engaged through ambiguity.

## Part 3: Episode Blueprint

### Structure: The "Build-Up" Episode (~5 minutes)

**Theme**: عائلة أحمد (Ahmad's family)

**Target new word**: جَدّ (grandfather) — assumed NOT in the learner's 800-word list

**Prerequisite known words used**: أب (father), أم (mother), كبير (big/old), عائلة (family), بيت (house), يحب (loves), يعرف (knows), قديم (old), رجل (man), سنة (year), كثير (many), ولد (boy/son), صغير (small/young), يذهب (goes), كل (every), يوم (day), هل (question particle), نعم (yes), جداً (very), لكن (but), أيضاً (also), هذا (this)

---

#### Phase 1: Establish Context with Pure Known Vocabulary (60 seconds)

*The teacher speaks slowly, with warmth. Every word is known.*

> أحمد عنده عائلة.
> عائلة أحمد كبيرة.
> عنده أب وأم.
> أبوه رجل طيب.
> وأمه... أمه طيبة أيضاً.
> أحمد يحب عائلته كثيراً.

*Pause. Then a question to engage:*

> هل تعرف عائلة أحمد؟
> الآن... ستعرف عائلة أحمد.

**What the listener experiences**: Pure comprehension. Every word lands. They're relaxed, following the simple narrative. They understand that Ahmad has a big family with a nice father and mother.

---

#### Phase 2: Introduce the New Word via Circumlocution (90 seconds)

*Now we prepare the ground for "جَدّ" without saying it yet.*

> أبو أحمد... عنده أب أيضاً.
> نعم! أبو الأب.
> أبو الأب رجل كبير في السنّ.
> عنده سنوات كثيرة... ستون سنة... سبعون سنة.
> رجل كبير، كبير جداً في السنّ.

*Now introduce the word:*

> هذا الرجل... أبو الأب... اسمه بالعربية: الجَدّ.
> الجَدّ.
> الجَدّ هو أبو الأب.

*Circling pattern (TPRS-adapted for audio):*

> هل الجَدّ هو أبو الأم؟
> *(pause)*
> لا! الجَدّ هو أبو الأب.
> وأبو الأم... هو جَدّ أيضاً!
> جَدّ أحمد رجل كبير في السنّ.
> هل جَدّ أحمد صغير؟
> *(pause)*
> لا! جَدّ أحمد كبير. كبير في السنّ.

**What the listener experiences**: By the time they hear "جَدّ", they already know it means "father's father" from context. The circling confirms and reinforces. The word has appeared 7 times in different frames.

---

#### Phase 3: Build Complexity — Rephrase and Expand (90 seconds)

*Now use the new word in increasingly rich sentences, always with known vocabulary.*

> جَدّ أحمد يسكن في بيت قديم.
> بيته قديم لكنه جميل.
> أحمد يذهب إلى بيت جَدّه كل يوم جمعة.
> كل يوم جمعة، أحمد يذهب إلى جَدّه.
> يعني... كل أسبوع، أحمد يزور جَدّه.

*Add emotional color:*

> أحمد يحب جَدّه كثيراً.
> ولماذا يحب جَدّه؟
> لأن جَدّه يحكي له قصصاً.
> *(pause)*
> قصص... يعني حكايات... كلام جميل عن أيام قديمة.
> الجَدّ يحكي والولد يسمع.
> أحمد يسمع ويسمع ويسمع.
> يحب أن يسمع قصص جَدّه.

**What the listener experiences**: The new word "جَدّ" is now woven into a narrative. They understand a paragraph about Ahmad visiting his grandfather every Friday to hear stories. "قصص" (stories) and "يحكي" (tells) may also be new — but they're explained in-line through the synonym "حكايات" and the functional description "كلام جميل عن أيام قديمة" (beautiful words about old days). The technique of explaining a word mid-flow without breaking stride.

---

#### Phase 4: The Passage — Putting It All Together (60 seconds)

*Now deliver a mini-passage at slightly faster (but still slow) speed. No English. No pauses for explanation. This is the payoff — can the listener follow a connected narrative?*

> أحمد ولد صغير. عنده عائلة كبيرة.
> أبوه رجل طيب وأمه طيبة.
> لكن أحمد يحب جَدّه أكثر من كل الناس.
> كل يوم جمعة، يذهب أحمد إلى بيت جَدّه القديم.
> يجلس مع جَدّه ويسمع قصصاً عن أيام قديمة.
> جَدّه رجل كبير في السنّ، لكن قلبه شاب.
> يعني... جَدّه كبير لكنه يضحك كثيراً ويحب الحياة.
> أحمد يقول: "جَدّي أحسن رجل في العالم."

**What the listener experiences**: A flowing, connected 8-sentence passage in Arabic that they can follow entirely. The passage contains the new word "جَدّ" (which they now know), plus a few bonus new words (قلبه شاب, يضحك, العالم) that are partially explained by context. This is the "moderately complex passage" endpoint. It would have been incomprehensible 4 minutes ago, but after the scaffolded build-up, it lands.

---

#### Phase 5: Replay and Close (30 seconds)

*Replay the full passage one more time, slightly faster. Then a warm close.*

> *(replay Phase 4 passage)*
>
> ممتاز! الآن تعرف كلمة "جَدّ"!
> وتعرف عائلة أحمد.
> في المرة القادمة... ستعرف المزيد.
> إلى اللقاء!

---

### Episode Timing Breakdown

| Phase | Duration | % Known Words | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------------|---------|
| 1. Establish context | 60s | 100% | Build confidence, set scene |
| 2. Introduce + circle | 90s | ~92% (new word + circling) | Teach through circumlocution |
| 3. Build complexity | 90s | ~88% (new word in rich contexts) | Deepen understanding |
| 4. Full passage | 60s | ~85% (passage with bonus words) | Test comprehension, payoff |
| 5. Replay + close | 30s | 100% | Reinforce, celebrate |
| **Total** | **~5.5 min** | | |

## Part 4: LLM Generation Prompt

```
You are an Arabic teacher creating a comprehensible input podcast episode. You speak ONLY in Arabic (MSA/fusha, full tashkeel). Your tone is warm, patient, and encouraging — like a beloved teacher talking one-on-one with a student.

## EPISODE PARAMETERS
- Topic: {topic}
- Target new word(s) to teach: {target_words_with_glosses}
- Episode duration target: ~5 minutes of spoken Arabic

## LEARNER'S KNOWN VOCABULARY
The learner knows these ~800 words reliably. Use ONLY these words plus standard function words (هل، في، من، إلى، على، و، أو، لكن، لأن، هذا، هذه، الذي، التي، كل، بعض، أيضاً، جداً، نعم، لا، ماذا، أين، لماذا، متى، كيف، الآن، ثم، هنا، هناك). Any word NOT on this list is unknown to the learner unless you explicitly teach it in the episode.

{known_word_list}

## EPISODE STRUCTURE (5 phases)

### Phase 1: Establish Context (8-10 sentences, ~60 seconds spoken)
- Use ONLY known vocabulary. Zero new words.
- Set up the topic/scene so the learner is oriented.
- End with a warm question or anticipation hook: هل تعرف...؟ الآن ستعرف!
- Purpose: build confidence, activate relevant vocabulary in the listener's mind.

### Phase 2: Introduce Target Word(s) via Circumlocution (10-14 sentences, ~90 seconds)
- Before saying the new word, describe it using 2-3 sentences of known words.
- The description should be so clear that the learner could guess the word.
- Then introduce the word: هذا هو... / اسمه بالعربية...
- Immediately circle the word (TPRS technique): ask yes/no questions, either/or, wh-questions, each time restating the answer.
- The new word should appear 6-8 times across different sentence frames.
- All surrounding vocabulary must be known.

### Phase 3: Build Complexity (10-14 sentences, ~90 seconds)
- Use the new word in increasingly rich sentences.
- Each sentence adds one new detail (adjective, time expression, reason clause).
- Use scaffolded rephrasing: say the same idea 3-4 ways, each slightly more complex.
- You may introduce 1-2 bonus words here, but ALWAYS explain them inline:
  - Using a simpler synonym: كلمة صعبة... يعني... كلمة سهلة
  - Using a description: قصص... يعني كلام جميل عن أيام قديمة
- Connect sentences into a flowing mini-narrative, not isolated examples.

### Phase 4: The Full Passage (6-10 sentences, ~60 seconds)
- Deliver a connected passage at slightly faster speed (still clear, not full native speed).
- No mid-sentence explanations. No English. No pauses for glossing.
- This passage should feel like a "real" piece of Arabic the learner can follow.
- It should use the new word 2-3 times naturally.
- Target: ~85% known vocabulary. The 15% unknown should be guessable from context.
- This is the "payoff" — the learner realizes they can follow a real Arabic paragraph.

### Phase 5: Replay and Close (2-3 sentences, ~30 seconds)
- Repeat the Phase 4 passage exactly (for re-processing on second listen).
- A brief celebratory close: ممتاز! الآن تعرف كلمة X.
- A teaser for next time: في المرة القادمة...
- Sign off: إلى اللقاء!

## CRITICAL RULES
1. NEVER use English. The entire episode is Arabic. The only exception: a 1-sentence English intro may be prepended by the app ("Welcome to your Arabic listening episode").
2. EVERY non-function word must either be in the known vocabulary list OR be explicitly taught/explained in the episode.
3. Use full tashkeel (diacritics) on all Arabic text.
4. Keep sentences SHORT in Phases 1-2 (3-6 words). Gradually lengthen in Phase 3 (5-10 words). Phase 4 can have longer sentences (8-15 words).
5. The warm teacher persona is essential. Use: يعني (that is to say), الآن (now), اسمع/اسمعي (listen), ممتاز (excellent), أحسنت (well done), هل تعرف (do you know?).
6. For circumlocution, prefer: category + description, contrast (ليس X بل Y), function/purpose, or simple synonym over abstract definitions.
7. Each phase should flow naturally into the next — no meta-commentary like "now we move to phase 3."
```

## Part 5: Implementation Notes (for future coding)

### How this maps to the existing podcast system

The current `podcast_service.py` uses a `Seg` dataclass with `ar()`, `ar_slow()`, `ar_normal()`, `en()`, and `silence()` helpers. An Arabic-in-Arabic episode would use almost exclusively `ar_slow()` and `ar_normal()` segments, with:
- Phase 1-2: `ar_slow()` (speed 0.7)
- Phase 3: `ar()` (speed 0.75)
- Phase 4: `ar_normal()` (speed 0.9)
- Phase 5: `ar_normal()` replay + `ar_slow()` closing
- One `en()` segment at the very start: "Welcome to your Arabic listening practice."
- `silence(2000)` between phases, `silence(1000)` between sentences in phases 1-3, `silence(600)` in phase 4

### Word selection for target words

Good candidates for target words in early episodes:
- Concrete nouns with easy circumlocution (جَدّ, جَدّة, مطبخ, حديقة, مكتبة)
- Verbs that can be demonstrated through scenario (يطبخ, يزرع, ينظف)
- Adjectives with clear contrast pairs (واسع/ضيق, عميق/قليل)

Bad candidates:
- Abstract nouns (حرية, عدالة) — too hard to circumlocute with 800 words
- Function words — not worth a whole episode
- Words too similar to known words — confusion risk outweighs benefit

### Episode sequencing

Episodes should follow **narrow listening chains**: episodes 1-3 on "family" (جَدّ, جَدّة, عَمّ), then episodes 4-6 on "the house" (مطبخ, حديقة, سطح), with family vocabulary carrying forward. This maximizes vocabulary recycling across episodes.

### Verification pipeline

After LLM generation, the same vocabulary compliance check used by `story_service.py` should verify that every non-function word either:
1. Exists in the learner's known vocabulary, OR
2. Was explicitly taught (circumlocuted) in a prior phase of the same episode

This requires parsing the episode phases in order: words taught in Phase 2 become "known" for the purposes of checking Phases 3-5.
