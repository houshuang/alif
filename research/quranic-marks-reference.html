حُرُوفُ القُرْآنِ

Marks of the Quran

The Uthmani script carries a layer of orthographic marks not found in modern Arabic printing. These small signs, refined over centuries of scholarly tradition, guide the reciter's voice: where to pause, how to nasalize, which silent letters are implied. For the learner, recognizing them transforms a wall of unfamiliar glyphs into a readable, pronounceable text.

Section I

عَلَامَاتُ الحُرُوف Vowel & Letter Marks

These marks indicate letters that are implied in the original consonantal skeleton (rasm) but not written as full letters. They were added by later scholars to preserve the correct reading of the Quran.

ـٰ
U+0670
Dagger Alif أَلِف خَنْجَرِيَّة
A small superscript alif indicating an implied long a vowel that is not written as a standalone alif in the Uthmani rasm. This is one of the most common Quranic marks — you will encounter it in the very first verse of Al-Fatihah. It replaces a full alif letter that would appear in modern standard spelling.
بِسْمِ ٱللَّهِ ٱلرَّحْمٰنِ ٱلرَّحِيمِ
Al-Fatihah 1:1
The alif in "Rahman" is written as a dagger alif above the meem, not as a standalone letter. Modern spelling: الرَّحْمَان. Uthmani: الرَّحْمٰن.
ـۥ
U+06E5
Small Waw وَاو صَغِيرَة
A miniature waw placed above or beside a letter to indicate an implied long u vowel. Like the dagger alif, it marks a vowel letter present in pronunciation but absent from the base consonantal text. Much less common than the dagger alif.
يَدْعُوۥا۟ لَمَن ضَرُّهُۥٓ أَقْرَبُ مِن نَّفْعِهِ
Al-Hajj 22:13
The small waw after the damma on the 'ayn signals that this is a long /u:/ vowel in the recitation, preserving the reading where the rasm is ambiguous.
ـۦ
U+06E6
Small Ya يَاء صَغِيرَة
A miniature ya indicating an implied long i vowel not written in the Uthmani rasm. Completes the set of three small vowel-letter signs (alif, waw, ya) that scholars added to mark the full vowel inventory.
وَٱلسَّمَآءَ بَنَيْنٰهَا بِأَيْىۦدٍ وَإِنَّا لَمُوسِعُونَ
Adh-Dhariyat 51:47
In "bi-aydin" (with strength), the small ya marks an implied long vowel in the Uthmani spelling that differs from the modern orthographic convention.
ـ۟
U+06DF
Small High Rounded Zero سُكُون مُسْتَدِير
The Uthmani variant of sukun — a small open circle placed above a letter instead of the standard head-of-kha (ْ) used in modern Arabic printing. Both marks mean the same thing: the letter carries no vowel. You will see this circle throughout Quran mushafs while modern textbooks use the jazm-style sukun.
قُلْ هُوَ ٱللَّهُ أَحَدٌ
Al-Ikhlas 112:1
The lam in "qul" (say) carries no vowel — in Uthmani script this is shown with the rounded zero rather than the angular sukun of modern print.
ـۢ
U+06E2
Small High Meem مِيم صَغِيرَة
A tiny meem placed above a noon or tanwin to indicate iqlab — the noon's sound transforms into a meem sound before the letter ba. The nasal quality (ghunnah) is held for approximately two beats. This is both a pronunciation guide and a tajweed rule marker.
مِنۢ بَعْدِ مَا جَآءَتْهُمُ ٱلْبَيِّنٰتُ
Al-Baqarah 2:213
The noon in "min" assimilates into a meem sound before "ba'di" — pronounced as "mim-ba'di" with a nasal hum. The small meem above makes this explicit.
ـۨ
U+06E8
Small High Noon نُون صَغِيرَة
A small noon placed above a letter, used in some Quranic orthographic traditions to indicate a noon sound. Less common than the small high meem, it appears in specific recitation variants and certain mushaf editions to mark nasalization or noon-related pronunciation features.
Used in specific orthographic traditions of the Uthmani script.
This mark is rarer than its meem counterpart and appears primarily in certain regional mushaf printings.
ـ۪
U+06EA
Empty Centre Low Stop دَائِرَة خَالِيَة سُفْلِيَّة
A small circle placed below a letter marking where a hamzah was historically present in older recitation traditions but has been dropped or softened in the standard reading. It is an orthographic fossil — a trace of how the pronunciation evolved between the time of revelation and the codification of the Uthmani script.
Historical orthographic marker in the Uthmani rasm.
This mark preserves historical phonological information. When you see it, it tells you that earlier Arabic speakers pronounced a hamzah at that position.
ـۜ
U+06DC
Small High Seen سِين صَغِيرَة
A miniature seen placed above a letter (usually a sad) to indicate that a seen reading applies in this position. This reflects variant readings (qira'at) where some reciters read a sin where others read a sad. The small seen signals "this letter may also be read as sin."
بِسۜطَةً
Al-Baqarah 2:247
The small seen above the sad in "bastatan" indicates that some recitation traditions read this word with a sin instead of a sad.
Section II

عَلَامَاتُ الوَقْف Waqf — Pause Signs

Waqf marks appear between words and tell the reciter when to stop, pause, or continue. Stopping at the wrong place can change the meaning of a verse, so these signs are carefully placed by Quran scholars. Learning them is essential for correct recitation.

U+06D6
Waqf Lazim
Compulsory stop. The meaning of the verse requires you to stop here.
Must stop

U+06DA
Waqf Wajib
Mandatory stop. If you continue, the meaning changes or becomes distorted.

U+06D7
Waqf Ja'iz

U+06D9
Waqf Mu'anaqah
Embracing stop. These come in pairs: if you stop at one, you must continue through the other. Choose one.

Preferred continuation. It is better to keep reading, but stopping is permitted if you need breath.

Section III

الرَّسْمُ العُثْمَانِي Uthmani vs Modern Spelling

The Quranic text preserves the spelling conventions of the original Uthmani codex (circa 650 CE), which differ from modern Arabic orthography (imla'i) in several systematic ways. Recognizing these differences prevents confusion when a word "looks wrong" — it is simply spelled the old way.

Feature Modern (Imla'i) Uthmani Notes
Alif deletion with dagger alif الرَّحْمَان الرَّحْمٰن The full alif is dropped and replaced with a dagger alif (ٰ) above the preceding letter. Extremely common.
Ta marbuta → ta maftouha رَحْمَة رَحْمَت Certain words use an open ta (ت) where modern Arabic uses ta marbuta (ة). Example: رَحْمَتَ ٱللَّهِ in the Quran vs رَحْمَةُ ٱللَّهِ in modern print.
Extended alif لَا لَآ Some positions receive an extra alif or a madda sign where modern spelling does not require one.
Ya vs alif maqsura مُوسَى مُوسٰى Some proper nouns are spelled differently. The dagger alif may also appear alongside the alif maqsura.
Word splitting أَيْنَمَا أَيْنَ مَا Some compound words that are joined in modern Arabic are written as two separate words in the Uthmani rasm.
Noon assimilation marked مِنْ بَعْدِ مِنۢ بَعْدِ The noon-to-meem assimilation (iqlab) before ba is explicitly marked with a small high meem, making the tajweed rule visible in the script.
Section IV

أَلْوَانُ التَّجْوِيد Tajweed Color Conventions

Color-coded mushafs use a standardized palette to highlight tajweed rules directly in the text. Each color signals a specific pronunciation rule, making it possible to read with correct tajweed even before memorizing all the rules. These colors follow the convention used in most color-coded Quran editions (such as the Tajweed Quran by Dar al-Ma'rifa).

Idgham
إِدْغَام
Assimilation. A noon sakinah or tanwin merges into one of six following letters, often with nasalization (ghunnah). The noon disappears into the next letter.
Letters: ي ن م و ل ر
مَن يَعْمَلْ
Qalqalah
قَلْقَلَة
Echo or bounce. When one of five specific letters carries a sukun, it produces a slight bouncing sound — a brief, sharp release rather than a smooth stop.
Letters: ق ط ب ج د
ٱقْرَأْ • يَجْعَل
Ikhfa
إِخْفَاء
Concealment. A noon sakinah or tanwin is partially hidden — not fully pronounced, not fully assimilated — producing a nasal hum before 15 specific letters.
Letters: ت ث ج د ذ ز س ش ص ض ط ظ ف ق ك
مِنْ ثَمَرَة
Ghunnah
غُنَّة
Nasalization. A held nasal sound lasting approximately two beats, produced through the nose. Occurs with doubled (mushaddad) noon and meem, and in many idgham and ikhfa contexts.
Letters: نّ مّ
إِنَّ ٱللَّهَ
Iqlab
إِقْلَاب
Conversion. A noon sakinah or tanwin immediately before the letter ba transforms into a meem sound, held with ghunnah for two beats. This is explicitly marked in the Uthmani script with the small high meem (ۢ).
Before: ب only
مِنۢ بَعْدِ
Izhar
إِظْهَار
Clear pronunciation. A noon sakinah or tanwin is pronounced clearly and distinctly before the six throat letters, with no nasalization, assimilation, or concealment.
Letters: ء ه ع ح غ خ
مَنْ آمَنَ
Madd
مَدّ
Elongation. A vowel is stretched beyond its normal length — from 2 up to 6 beats depending on the type. Obligatory madd occurs before hamzah or sukun; natural madd is the default 2-beat stretch of any long vowel.
Vowels: ـَا ـُو ـِي
قَالُوٓا۟ سُبْحٰنَكَ
بِسْمِ ٱللَّهِ ٱلرَّحْمٰنِ ٱلرَّحِيمِ
