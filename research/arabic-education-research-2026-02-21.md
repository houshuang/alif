## 1. International Assessments

### 1.1 PIRLS 2021 (Progress in International Reading Literacy Study)

**Citation:** Mullis, I.V.S., von Davier, M., Foy, P., Fishbein, B., Reynolds, K.A., & Wry, E. (2023). *PIRLS 2021 International Results in Reading*. Boston College, TIMSS & PIRLS International Study Center.

**URL:** https://pirls2021.org/results/achievement/overall/

**Full report PDF:** https://pirls2021.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/files/PIRLS-2021-International-Results-in-Reading.pdf

**What it measures:** Reading comprehension achievement in 4th-grade students (age 9-10) across 57 countries and 8 benchmarking entities. The PIRLS scale centerpoint is 500 (set to the 2001 international mean), with a standard deviation of 100.

**Arab country scores (PIRLS 2021):**

| Country | Score | Relation to Centerpoint (500) |
|---------|-------|-------------------------------|
| Qatar | 485 | Below (but close) |
| UAE (national) | 483 | Below |
| Dubai (benchmarking) | 552 | Significantly above |
| Abu Dhabi (benchmarking) | 440 | Significantly below |
| Bahrain | 458 | Below |
| Saudi Arabia | 449 | Below |
| Oman | 429 | Significantly below |
| Jordan | 381 | Significantly below |
| Egypt | 378 | Significantly below |
| Morocco | 372 | Significantly below |

**Key findings:**
- Every Arab country that participated scored below the PIRLS centerpoint of 500, except Dubai as a benchmarking entity (552).
- Egypt and Morocco scored at the very bottom of all participating countries globally, more than 120 points below the centerpoint.
- The gulf between Dubai private schools (566) and national averages in Egypt/Morocco (~375) represents nearly 200 scale points -- roughly two standard deviations.
- Girls outperformed boys in reading across all participating Arab countries.
- Some countries (Bahrain, UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia) administered the test in fall 2021 rather than spring, which may affect comparability.

**Notes on the UAE:** The UAE result is complex. The national average (483) masks enormous variation: Dubai private schools scored 566 (top 10 globally, comparable to Finland), while Abu Dhabi scored 440. Private schools in Sharjah approached scores of Germany and France. This reflects the stratification between well-resourced private education serving expatriate populations and national public education.

## 2. Learning Poverty and Literacy Statistics

### 2.1 World Bank Learning Poverty Data

**Citation:** World Bank (2022). *The State of Global Learning Poverty: 2022 Update*. Washington, DC: World Bank Group.

**URL:** https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2022/06/23/70-of-10-year-olds-now-in-learning-poverty-unable-to-read-and-understand-a-simple-text

**Definition:** "Learning poverty" = the share of 10-year-old children who cannot read and understand a simple, age-appropriate text.

**Key statistics:**
- **MENA regional learning poverty rate: ~60% pre-COVID** (59% was the widely cited pre-pandemic figure).
- Post-COVID estimates suggest this has risen by at least 10 percentage points, potentially reaching 70%.
- Globally, 70% of 10-year-olds are now in learning poverty (up from 57% pre-COVID).
- MENA performs worse than the global average despite higher income levels than Sub-Saharan Africa or South Asia.

**Country learning poverty briefs:** https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/education/brief/country-learning-poverty-briefs

**Significance:** The 60% learning poverty figure means that in a typical MENA classroom, 6 out of 10 children cannot read and understand a basic text at the end of primary school. This is remarkable given that MENA countries spend a higher share of GDP on education than many other developing regions.

## 3. The Arabic Literacy Crisis: Major Reports

### 3.1 World Bank: Advancing Arabic Language Teaching and Learning (2021)

**Citation:** Gregory, L., & Blom, A. (2021). *Advancing Arabic Language Teaching and Learning: A Path to Reducing Learning Poverty in the Middle East and North Africa*. Washington, DC: World Bank Group.

**Full report PDF:** https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/909741624654308046/pdf/Advancing-Arabic-Language-Teaching-and-Learning-A-Path-to-Reducing-Learning-Poverty-in-the-Middle-East-and-North-Africa.pdf

**Press release:** https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/press-release/2021/06/28/new-path-to-address-mena-learning-crisis-through-advancing-arabic-language-teaching-and-learning

**This is the single most important recent report on Arabic education.** Key findings:

1. **The core problem:** More than half of MENA children cannot read and understand an age-appropriate text by age 10.

2. **Why Arabic teaching fails -- the report identifies these structural issues:**
   - Children arrive at school with minimal MSA exposure. Parents in MENA are less likely to read to young children or play word games compared to parents in other regions.
   - Enrollment in formal early childhood education (preschool) is lower in MENA than in other regions.
   - Children are taught to read and write "in a rigid way with a focus on rules, grammar, and accuracy" with "a lack of playfulness and inquiry."
   - Many Arabic language teachers "are often the product of ineffective Arabic language education and are not comfortable using it as a medium of instruction."
   - There is no explicit strategy for bridging the gap between spoken dialects and MSA.

3. **The 9-step framework for improvement:**
   - Develop national literacy strategies with quantifiable goals
   - Increase early exposure to MSA
   - Ensure language-rich environments
   - Apply the science of reading to Arabic instruction
   - Maximize overlap between MSA and colloquial varieties
   - Improve teacher education and professional development
   - Develop better curriculum materials
   - Strengthen assessment and early intervention
   - Support schools with resources and accountability

4. **Country progress:** Jordan, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE have prepared specific strategies for improving Arabic literacy with ambitious learning targets. Morocco piloted a "Teaching at the Right Level" approach showing substantial improvements in Arabic, Mathematics, and French.

**Follow-up publication:** Blom, A. (2023). "The World Bank's Approach to Reducing Learning Poverty in the Middle East and North Africa: Advancing Arabic Language." *Arab Journal of Applied Linguistics*.

**URL:** https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1410089.pdf

**Citation:** UNDP (2002). *Arab Human Development Report 2002: Creating Opportunities for Future Generations*. New York: United Nations Publications.

**URL:** https://www.undp.org/arab-states/publications/arab-human-development-report-2002

**URL:** https://hdr.undp.org/content/arab-human-development-report-2003

**Overview of all reports:** https://www.europarl.europa.eu/meetdocs/2004_2009/documents/dv/overview_/overview_en.pdf

**These reports were landmark publications that defined the discourse on Arab development deficits.**

**AHDR 2002 -- The Three Deficits:**
The first report identified three major "development deficits" holding the Arab region back:
1. **Knowledge** -- a growing knowledge gap with the rest of the world
2. **Women's empowerment** -- gender inequality constraining development
3. **Freedom** -- governance deficits limiting human potential

**AHDR 2003 -- Building a Knowledge Society (key statistics):**
- The number of books translated into Arabic increased from 175/year (1970-1975) to 330/year -- but this was still one-fifth of what Greece alone translated.
- The aggregate total of all books translated into Arabic from the 9th century Abbasid era (al-Ma'mun) to the present day amounts to approximately 10,000 books -- equivalent to what Spain translates in a single year.
- Books translated per million people: Arab world 4.4, Hungary 519, Spain 920.
- 65 million adults in the Arab region were illiterate, almost two-thirds of them women.
- The quality of education "has deteriorated severely."
- There is a "severe mismatch between the labour market and the education system."

**Strategic vision (five pillars):**
1. Guaranteeing key freedoms
2. Disseminating quality education
3. Embedding science
4. Shifting towards knowledge-based production
5. Developing an enlightened Arab knowledge model

## 4. Diglossia and Its Impact on Education

### 4.1 Queen Rania Foundation Systematic Review (2021)

**Citation:** Queen Rania Foundation (2021). *A Report on the Effect of Arabic Language Diglossia on Teaching and Learning*. Amman: QRF.

**Full report PDF:** https://qrf.org/sites/default/files/2023-11/report_on_the_effect_of_arabic_language_diglossia_on_teaching_and_learning_0.pdf

**Summary page:** https://www.qrf.org/en/research-reports/diglossia-research

**Blog posts on the review:**
- https://qrf.org/en/media-center/our-latest-blogs/raising-our-heads-documents-sharing-some-mid-term-findings-our
- https://www.qrf.org/en/media-center/our-latest-blogs/significance-diglossia
- https://www.qrf.org/en/media-center/our-latest-blogs/arabic-language-diglossia-systematic-review

**This is the most comprehensive systematic review of diglossia research to date.**

**Key findings:**
- **The evidence is contradictory and inconclusive**: "There is no clear understanding about the impact diglossia has on teaching and learning that is fully based on evidence."
- Difficulty in learning MSA diminishes with increased exposure and grade advancement, with improvement evident around Grade 4.
- Early literacy activities at home sometimes provide students with baseline MSA knowledge before formal schooling.
- The distance between MSA and local dialects creates measurable challenges, compounded by socioeconomic factors.
- Using the "Simple View of Reading" framework, skilled decoders may understand letter-sounds but struggle with comprehension when dialect-MSA gaps are substantial.

**Recommended bridging strategies:**
- Early MSA exposure through multimedia
- Diglossic-awareness activities
- Explicit vocabulary instruction
- Parental/community involvement
- Adapted methodologies for non-native speakers

**Context:** Lebanon and Jordan contributed the majority of diglossia research studies. The review was conducted by researchers at Zayed University (UAE).

### 4.2 Saiegh-Haddad: Linguistic Distance and Reading Acquisition (2003)

**Citation:** Saiegh-Haddad, E. (2003). Linguistic distance and initial reading acquisition: The case of Arabic diglossia. *Applied Psycholinguistics*, 24(3), 431-451.

**URL:** https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/applied-psycholinguistics/article/abs/linguistic-distance-and-initial-reading-acquisition-the-case-of-arabic-diglossia/9E475E6D4A634E683830D1FCE0E501AB

**ResearchGate:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/231894994_Linguistic_Distance_and_Initial_Reading_Acquisition_The_Case_of_Arabic_Diglossia

**Author:** Elinor Saiegh-Haddad, Full Professor at Bar-Ilan University, is the leading researcher on Arabic diglossia and reading acquisition.

**Scholar profile:** https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=Z976BOwAAAAJ&hl=en

**Key findings:**
- Examined two diglossic variables -- phoneme and word syllabic structure -- by testing children's phoneme isolation and pseudoword decoding skills.
- Both diglossic variables interfered with children's performance on both tasks in both grades tested.
- Linguistic distance between spoken Arabic and MSA directly impacts children's phonological processing, phonological and morphological awareness, and Standard Arabic word reading.
- This paper laid the theoretical and methodological grounds for studying the psycholinguistics of diglossia.

**Why this matters:** This foundational paper empirically demonstrated what had been theoretically suspected: that diglossia is not merely a sociolinguistic curiosity but has measurable cognitive consequences for reading acquisition. Children must essentially learn to read in what is functionally a second language.

### 4.4 Frontiers in Education: Non-Deficit Model (2025)

**Citation:** (2025). Arabic diglossia: Advocating for a non-deficit model in comparative analysis of reading and language acquisition. *Frontiers in Education*, 10, 1518728.

**URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2025.1518728/full

**Key argument:**
- Challenges the prevailing "deficit model" that treats diglossia as inherently problematic.
- Argues for comparative analysis that recognizes diglossia as a linguistic reality requiring adapted pedagogy rather than viewing it as a disorder.
- Diglossia has a negative impact on "phonological awareness, morphological awareness, phonological representation, word decoding, reading fluency, and letter naming."
- This negative impact is greater for families of lower socioeconomic standing.

**Citation:** (Referenced in QRF systematic review and other studies)

## 5. Arabic Orthography, Morphology, and Reading Acquisition

### 5.1 Orthographic Depth and Arabic

**Citation:** Abu-Rabia, S. (2016). Deep and shallow in Arabic orthography: New evidence from reading performance of elementary school native Arab readers.

**URL:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/294891709_Deep_and_shallow_in_Arabic_orthography_New_evidence_from_reading_performance_of_elementary_school_native_Arab_readers

**Full text PDF:** https://zai.zu.ac.ae/zairesources/651362/26e740a5-8021-4b0d-a0b4-f5be4c718a22.pdf

**Key concepts:**
- Arabic has a unique **dual orthographic system**: vowelized (shallow/transparent) and unvowelized (deep/opaque).
- Unlike Roman scripts where each written language has one depth, Arabic operates at two depths simultaneously.
- **Vowelized Arabic** (with tashkeel/diacritics) is a transparent orthography: each phoneme maps predictably to a grapheme. This is used for beginning readers and religious texts.
- **Unvowelized Arabic** (without tashkeel) is a deep orthography: readers must infer short vowels from context, morphological knowledge, and syntactic parsing.
- The transition from vowelized to unvowelized text (typically around end of elementary school) creates a second major hurdle in reading development.
- Unvowelized Arabic produces a "high amount of homographs" -- the reader must rely on context to determine both pronunciation and meaning.

**Practical relevance:** This dual nature is why tashkeel fading (gradually removing diacritics as proficiency increases) is pedagogically sound. It mirrors the natural transition Arab children make during schooling.

---

### 5.2 Role of Diacritics (Tashkeel) in Reading

**Citation:** Abu-Rabia, S. Reading in Arabic: New Evidence for the Role of Vowel Signs.

**URL:** https://zai.zu.ac.ae/zairesources/651362/eaeca72f-d0d9-415d-9e15-85fa681a51fa.pdf

**Key findings:**
- Diacritical marks significantly affect reading difficulty.
- Vowelized Arabic is considerably more transparent and easier for beginning readers.
- As readers gain proficiency, they increasingly rely on consonantal roots and morphological patterns rather than vowel marks.
- The ability to read unvowelized text is a key marker of Arabic reading proficiency.

---

### 5.3 Root-Based Morphology and Word Recognition

**Citation:** Boudelaa, S., & Marslen-Wilson, W.D. (Various). Reading in a root-based-morphology language: The case of Arabic.

**URL:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/227774899_Reading_in_a_root-based-morphology_language_The_case_of_Arabic

**Citation:** (2020). Early Sensitivity to Morphology in Beginning Readers of Arabic. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 11, 552315.

**URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2020.552315/full

**Citation:** (Development of root awareness measure). *Applied Psycholinguistics*.

**URL:** https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/applied-psycholinguistics/article/abs/development-of-a-measure-of-root-awareness-to-account-for-reading-performance-in-the-arabic-language-a-development-and-validation-study/787A072025D7A6E39508978A620BD68E

**Key findings:**
- Arabic word structure is "highly morphemic" -- almost all words derive from combining at least two bound morphemes (roots and patterns) simultaneously.
- Consonants play a "much greater role than vowels in word recognition."
- When reading unvowelized Arabic, readers focus on consonant structure and rely on morphological knowledge and sentence context.
- **Root awareness emerges earlier than word-pattern (wazn) awareness** in children's morphological development.
- Children read faster and more accurately when pseudowords are composed of frequent morphemes.
- Phonological awareness AND morphological awareness are both strong predictors of Arabic reading performance.
- Morphological awareness correlates significantly with both word-level reading and reading comprehension.

**Practical relevance:** This research supports pedagogical approaches that emphasize root-pattern awareness (wazn) as a strategy for vocabulary acquisition and reading fluency. Teaching learners to recognize root consonants and common patterns accelerates word recognition.

---

### 5.4 What Predicts Word Reading in Arabic?

**Citation:** (2023). What predicts word reading in Arabic? *Frontiers in Psychology*, 14, 1077643.

**URL:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1077643/full

**Key finding:** Multiple factors predict Arabic word reading, including phonological awareness, morphological awareness, and orthographic processing. The relative importance varies by age and reading experience level.

## 6. Arabic as a Foreign Language (AFL) Research

### 6.1 FSI Language Difficulty Classification

**Citation:** Foreign Service Institute, U.S. Department of State. Language difficulty rankings and training time estimates.

**URL (FSI):** https://www.state.gov/national-foreign-affairs-training-center/foreign-language-training

**URL (overview):** https://www.languagetesting.com/how-long-does-it-take

**URL (detailed):** https://www.fsi-language-courses.org/blog/fsi-language-difficulty/

**Key facts:**
- Arabic is classified as **Category IV** (or Category V in some taxonomies that split the hardest tier), alongside Chinese (Mandarin), Cantonese, Japanese, and Korean.
- Expected time to reach S-3/R-3 (ILR Level 3, roughly CEFR B2/C1): **88 weeks / 2,200 class hours** of intensive study.
- For comparison, Category I languages (Spanish, French, Italian) require 24-30 weeks / 600-750 hours.
- Arabic requires roughly **3x the study time** of a Romance language to reach the same proficiency level.
- The State Department provides incentive pay: 5% salary for 2/2 rating, 10% for 3/3, 15% for 4/4.

**National Academies review:** A Principled Approach to Language Assessment: Considerations for the U.S. Foreign Service Institute. https://www.nationalacademies.org/read/25748/chapter/4

**Why Arabic is so difficult for English speakers:**
1. Non-Latin script with right-to-left directionality
2. Root-and-pattern morphology (fundamentally different from English affixation)
3. Diglossia -- learners must decide which variety to study and navigate between them
4. Complex verb conjugation (10+ verb forms/patterns)
5. Case system (largely absent in spoken Arabic but present in MSA)
6. Phonemes that do not exist in English (pharyngeals, emphatics)
7. Orthographic depth when diacritics are absent

### 6.2 ACTFL Proficiency Guidelines for Arabic

**Citation:** American Council on the Teaching of Foreign Languages (ACTFL). (2012). ACTFL Proficiency Guidelines (Revised). Alexandria, VA: ACTFL.

**URL:** https://www.actfl.org/resources/actfl-proficiency-guidelines-2012/arabic

**Key information:**
- Four major levels: Novice, Intermediate, Advanced, Superior (each with Low/Mid/High subdivisions).

- Research from Cambridge suggests a ~40% failure rate in achieving target proficiency within recommended study periods for Category IV/V languages.

## 9. Spaced Repetition and Vocabulary Acquisition

### 9.1 SRS for Arabic Vocabulary: University Case Study

**Citation:** (2024). Introducing Spaced Repetition Software (SRS) for vocabulary acquisition in a university-level Arabic language course: A case study. *Journal of Language Teaching*.

**URL:** https://jlt.ac/home/article/view/99

**Full text:** https://jlt.ac/home/article/download/99/83/325

**Key findings:**
- Twenty-two beginning Arabic students were required to use SRS for vocabulary acquisition.
- Over 100 years of research confirms spaced repetition as superior to massed practice for long-term retention.
- Recommended pace: 5-10 new Arabic words per day, with the "compounding effect" meaning 50-100 reviews daily within a month.
- Students need to adhere to the rigorous schedule SRS requires.
- Self-monitoring of flashcard reviews helps students recognize the link between effort and outcome.

**Practical note:** Anki remains the gold standard for serious learners due to its algorithm and offline flexibility. Lingualism (https://lingualism.com/) provides pre-built Arabic Anki decks for MSA and various dialects.

## Summary of Key Takeaways

1. **The scale of the problem is enormous.** 60% of MENA children cannot read an age-appropriate text by age 10 (pre-COVID). International assessments (PIRLS, PISA) consistently show Arab countries scoring significantly below international averages in reading.

2. **Diglossia is a structural challenge with no consensus solution.** Children effectively learn to read in a language they do not speak at home. The QRF systematic review found contradictory evidence about whether this is a primary cause of poor outcomes or a manageable factor.

3. **Teaching methods are a major part of the problem.** The World Bank identifies rigid, grammar-focused, accuracy-obsessed approaches that lack playfulness and inquiry. Many Arabic teachers are themselves products of ineffective education.

4. **Arabic's orthographic system creates a unique double challenge.** Vowelized text is transparent and learnable, but the transition to unvowelized text requires deep morphological knowledge and context awareness. This transition point is a critical vulnerability in the education pipeline.

5. **Root-pattern morphology is both a challenge and an opportunity.** Research shows that morphological awareness (root and wazn awareness) is a strong predictor of reading success. Pedagogies that leverage this structure rather than treating Arabic as if it were English can be more effective.

6. **For foreign learners, Arabic is objectively one of the hardest languages.** The FSI's 2,200-hour estimate (vs. 600 for Spanish) is well-established. The combination of script, morphology, diglossia, and phonology creates compounding difficulty.

7. **Technology shows promise but lacks Arabic-specific research.** Most studies on language learning apps and gamification are not Arabic-specific. The technical challenges of Arabic NLP (morphological richness, orthographic ambiguity, dialectal variation) remain partially unsolved.

8. **Spaced repetition works for Arabic vocabulary** but requires discipline. The compounding review load is a common attrition factor. Sentence-context review (rather than isolated word cards) may improve retention and transfer.
