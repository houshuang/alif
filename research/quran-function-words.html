Function Words of the Quran

Arabic function words — particles, pronouns, prepositions, and conjunctions — are the connective tissue of Quranic Arabic. They appear on almost every line, yet each carries precise grammatical meaning. Mastering these ~100 words unlocks the structure of every verse you read.

Disconnected Object Pronouns

الضمائر المنفصلة المنصوبة

These are among the most theologically powerful words in the Quran. While normal object pronouns attach as suffixes (e.g. -hu "him", -ka "you"), the إيّا (iyyā-) forms stand alone as separate words. In Arabic grammar, this fronting creates exclusive emphasis: "You alone" — not just "you." When you hear إيّاكَ نعبد, the structure itself means worship belongs exclusively to God.

إِيَّاكَ نَعْبُدُ وَإِيَّاكَ نَسْتَعِينُ
iyyāka naʿbudu wa-iyyāka nastaʿīn
"You alone we worship, and You alone we ask for help."
This is verse 5 of Al-Fātiḥah (The Opening), recited by Muslims in every unit of prayer — making إيّاك possibly the most frequently spoken Arabic word in history. The normal word order would be نعبدك ("we worship you") with the pronoun as a suffix. By fronting إيّاك to the beginning, the verse creates حصر (ḥaṣr) — grammatical restriction — meaning "You and no one else."
Sūrat al-Fātiḥah (1:5)

💡 Why does fronting create exclusivity?

In Arabic, the default verb-subject-object order (VSO) is نعبدك — "we worship you." Moving the object before the verb (إيّاك نعبد) is grammatically marked. Arabic grammarians call this تقديم ما حقّه التأخير — "fronting what by right should come later." This marked structure signals that the fronted element is the exclusive focus. It's as if the sentence says: "As for You — that's who we worship, no one else." The same device is used in legal Arabic for exclusivity clauses.

The Mystery Letters

الحروف المقطّعة

Twenty-nine surahs of the Quran open with combinations of Arabic letters that are not words in any known sense. They are recited as individual letter names: alif, lām, mīm — not as a word. Their meaning has been debated for 1400 years. Some scholars say they are divine secrets; others suggest they are acoustic hooks that grab attention before revelation begins. No definitive explanation exists — which is itself part of their mystique.

الٓمٓ
alif lām mīm
Alif Lam Mim
Muqaṭṭaʿāt Quranic only
Opens 6 surahs including Al-Baqarah, Āl ʿImrān, and Al-ʿAnkabūt. The most frequent combination.
الٓمٓ ۚ ذَٰلِكَ الْكِتَابُ لَا رَيْبَ ۛ فِيهِ
"Alif Lam Mim. This is the Book about which there is no doubt."
Al-Baqarah (2:1–2)
الٓر
alif lām rā
Alif Lam Ra
Muqaṭṭaʿāt Quranic only
Opens 5 surahs: Yūnus, Hūd, Yūsuf, Ibrāhīm, and Al-Ḥijr.
حمٓ
ḥā mīm
Ha Mim
Muqaṭṭaʿāt Quranic only
Opens 7 consecutive surahs (40–46), known collectively as the "Ḥawāmīm" family.
طه
ṭā hā
Ta Ha
Muqaṭṭaʿāt Quranic only
Opens Surah 20 (Ṭā Hā). Some say it means "O man" in an ancient dialect. Often used as a name for Prophet Muhammad.
يسٓ

Ya Sin
Muqaṭṭaʿāt Quranic only
Opens Surah 36. Called "the Heart of the Quran." Some interpret it as "O human being."
صٓ

Sad
Muqaṭṭaʿāt Quranic only
A single letter opening Surah 38. Immediately followed by "By the Quran full of remembrance."

💡 Theories about the mystery letters

1. Divine secret — Many classical scholars (including Ibn ʿAbbās) said these are among the secrets of the Quran that God alone knows.
2. Attention device — The unexpected letter sounds interrupt the listener, creating attention before the message begins — like a clearing of the throat.
3. Proof of inimitability — "Here are the same letters you use every day, yet you cannot produce anything like this Book."
4. Surah names — Some may simply serve as names for their surahs, the way we might title a chapter "Q" or "X."

Prepositions

حروف الجرّ

Prepositions in Arabic (حروف الجرّ, ḥurūf al-jarr — literally "letters of pulling") put the following noun into the genitive case (مجرور). Some are standalone words; others are single-letter clitics that attach directly to the next word. The Quran's prepositions often carry theological weight — فِي (in) versus عَلَى (upon) can change the meaning of a verse significantly.

فِي
fī
In / within
Preposition All Arabic
One of the most common words in the Quran. Indicates location, time, or state.
وَفِي أَنفُسِكُمْ ۚ أَفَلَا تُبْصِرُونَ
"And within yourselves — will you not then see?"
Adh-Dhāriyāt (51:21)
مِنْ
min
From / of / some of
Preposition All Arabic
Indicates origin, partitive meaning ("some of"), or starting point. The most frequent preposition in the Quran.
خَلَقَ الْإِنسَانَ مِنْ عَلَقٍ
"Created man from a clinging substance"
Al-ʿAlaq (96:2)
عَلَى
ʿalā
On / upon / over
Preposition All Arabic
Physical or abstract "upon." Often expresses obligation or mastery.
خَتَمَ اللَّهُ عَلَىٰ قُلُوبِهِمْ
"God has set a seal upon their hearts"
Al-Baqarah (2:7)
إلَى
ilā
To / toward / until
Preposition All Arabic
Indicates direction, destination, or endpoint. Often paired with مِن for "from...to."
عَنْ
ʿan
About / away from
Preposition All Arabic
Indicates separation or topic. يسألونك عن — "They ask you about..."
مَعَ
maʿa
With / together with
Preposition All Arabic
Companionship or accompaniment. إنَّ اللهَ مَعَ الصابرين — "God is with the patient."
بَيْنَ
bayna
Between / among
Preposition All Arabic
Spatial or relational "between." Often doubled: بينهم — "among them."
حَتَّى
ḥattā
Until / even
Preposition All Arabic
Marks an endpoint. Can also mean "even" (inclusive emphasis). One of the few Arabic words that can be a preposition, conjunction, or particle.
عِنْدَ
ʿinda
At / in the possession of
Preposition All Arabic
Proximity or possession. عِندَ اللهِ — "with God / in God's sight."

Single-Letter Clitics

الحروف المتّصلة

These are the invisible architecture of Quranic Arabic. Single-letter prepositions and conjunctions attach directly to the following word with no space — بِسْمِ is actually بِ + اسْم ("in/by + name"). Recognizing these clitics is the single most important skill for reading the Quran, because almost every phrase contains at least one.

بِسْمِ اللَّهِ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ
bi-smi llāhi r-raḥmāni r-raḥīm
"In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful"
The Basmala — the most recited Arabic phrase on Earth. Notice: بِسْمِ is a contraction. The بِـ is the clitic preposition "in/by," attached to اسْم ("name"). When hamzat al-waṣl (ٱ) is preceded by a clitic, the initial alef drops entirely, leaving بِسْمِ instead of *بِاسْمِ.

in / by

name
+

God
بِـ

In / by / with
Clitic preposition All Arabic
The Swiss Army knife of Arabic prepositions: means "in" (بالمدينة — "in the city"), "by" (بالله — "by God"), or "with" (instrumental — بالسيف — "with the sword").
لِـ
li-
For / to / belonging to
Clitic preposition All Arabic
Indicates possession (لِلّه — "belonging to God"), purpose (لِيَعْلَمَ — "so that He knows"), or benefit (لَكُم — "for you").
لِإِيلَافِ قُرَيْشٍ
"For the accustomed security of the Quraysh"
Quraysh (106:1)
كَـ
ka-
Like / as
Clitic preposition All Arabic
Comparison. كَمَثَلِ — "like the example of." Very common in Quranic parables.
لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِ شَيْءٌ
"There is nothing like unto Him"
Ash-Shūrā (42:11)
وَ
wa-
And / by (oath)
Clitic conjunction All Arabic
As a conjunction, simply "and." The oath usage (وَالله) is common across all registers. But at the start of a verse/oath, it means "By...!" — وَالْفَجْرِ ("By the dawn!"). The most frequent word in the Quran.
وَالْعَصْرِ ۝ إِنَّ الْإِنسَانَ لَفِي خُسْرٍ
"By time! Indeed, mankind is in loss."
Al-ʿAṣr (103:1–2)
فَـ
fa-
So / then / and so
Clitic conjunction All Arabic
Unlike وَ (simple "and"), فَ implies sequence or consequence: "and then," "and so." فَسَجَدُوا — "and so they prostrated." Often marks cause→effect.

Conjunctions

حروف العطف

Arabic conjunctions (حروف العطف, ḥurūf al-ʿaṭf — "letters of coordination") link words, phrases, or clauses. Each carries a different nuance: وَ is neutral "and," فَ implies sequence, ثُمَّ implies a time gap, and بَلْ corrects or contradicts. The Quran uses these distinctions with precision.

ثُمَّ
thumma
Then / and then (after a delay)
Conjunction All Arabic
Unlike فَ (immediate sequence), ثُمَّ implies a gap in time. "God created you, thumma He will cause you to die" — creation and death are separated by a lifetime.
أَوْ
aw
Or
Conjunction All Arabic
Choice between alternatives. In legal verses, distinguishes options for expiation or worship.
لٰكِنَّ / لٰكِنْ
lākinna / lākin
But / however
Conjunction All Arabic
Adversative — introduces a contrast. لٰكِنَّ (with shadda) is a "sister of إنَّ" that takes a noun in the accusative. لٰكِنْ (without shadda) is a simple conjunction.
بَلْ
bal
Rather / nay / but instead
Conjunction Classical & Formal MSA
Correction or escalation. Negates what came before and replaces it. بَلْ هُمْ قَوْمٌ يَعْدِلُونَ — "Rather, they are a people who deviate."
أَنَّ / أَنْ
anna / an
That (content clause)
Conjunction Classical & Formal MSA
Introduces noun clauses. أَنَّ (with shadda): "that" after verbs of knowledge. أَنْ (without): "to" before subjunctive verbs. يُرِيدُ أَنْ يُخْرِجَكُم — "He wants to expel you."
إِنَّ
inna
Indeed / verily
Emphatic particle Classical & Formal MSA
The great emphasizer. Opens hundreds of Quranic statements. Puts the following noun in accusative. إِنَّ اللهَ عَزِيزٌ حَكِيمٌ — "Indeed God is Mighty, Wise."
إِنَّ مَعَ الْعُسْرِ يُسْرًا
"Indeed, with hardship comes ease."
Ash-Sharḥ (94:6)

Personal Pronouns

الضمائر المنفصلة

Arabic distinguishes 12 personal pronouns: 3 persons × 2 genders × 2 numbers (singular, plural), plus a dual form. These are the independent/subject forms. In the Quran, God refers to Himself as أنا (I), نحن (We — the royal "We," not plural), or in the third person هو (He). The choice carries theological meaning.

هُوَ
huwa
He / it
Pronoun — 3rd m. sg. All Arabic
Often refers to God. قُلْ هُوَ اللهُ أَحَدٌ — "Say: He is God, the One." Surah Al-Ikhlāṣ opens with this word.
هِيَ
hiya
She / it (f.)
Pronoun — 3rd f. sg. All Arabic
Also used for non-gendered "it" when referring to feminine nouns (most abstract nouns in Arabic are grammatically feminine).
هُمْ
hum
They (m.)
Pronoun — 3rd m. pl. All Arabic
Extremely frequent. أُولٰئِكَ هُمُ الْمُفْلِحُونَ — "Those are the successful ones."
نَحْنُ
naḥnu
We
Pronoun — 1st pl. All Arabic
When God says نَحْنُ, it is the royal "We" (تعظيم), not a plurality of gods. إِنَّا نَحْنُ نَزَّلْنَا الذِّكْرَ — "Indeed, it is We who sent down the Reminder."
أَنْتَ
anta
You (m. singular)
Pronoun — 2nd m. sg. All Arabic
Addresses a single male. Often addresses the Prophet: إِنَّكَ أَنْتَ العَلِيمُ — "Indeed, You are the All-Knowing."
أَنَا
anā
I
Pronoun — 1st sg. All Arabic
When God says أَنَا, it signals intimate, direct speech. إِنَّنِي أَنَا اللهُ — "Indeed, I am God" (Ṭā Hā 20:14).

Pronoun Suffixes

الضمائر المتّصلة

While independent pronouns stand alone, attached pronouns (الضمائر المتّصلة) fuse onto the end of verbs, nouns, and prepositions. They're the most compact way Arabic expresses "his," "their," "you," "us," etc. A single suffix turns كتاب (book) into كتابهم (their book) — no separate word needed. You'll see these on nearly every line of the Quran.

Suffix Transliteration Meaning Example Register
ـهُ -hu him / his / it رَبُّهُ — "his Lord" All
ـها -hā her / its أَهْلَها — "its people" All
ـهُمْ -hum them / their قُلُوبِهِمْ — "their hearts" All
ـهُنَّ -hunna them (f.) / their (f.) أَجَلُهُنَّ — "their (f.) term" Formal
ـهُما -humā them (dual) / their (dual) بَيْنَهُما — "between them two" Formal
ـكَ -ka you (m. sg.) / your رَبُّكَ — "your Lord" All
ـكُمْ -kum you (pl.) / your رَبُّكُمْ — "your (pl.) Lord" All
ـكُما -kumā you (dual) رَبُّكُما — "Lord of you two" Formal
ـنا -nā us / our رَبَّنا — "our Lord" All
ـي / ـنِي -ī / -nī me / my رَبِّي — "my Lord" All

💡 One word, three pieces of information

When a suffix attaches to a preposition + noun, you get a complete phrase in one written word: عَلَيْهِمْ = عَلَى (upon) + هِمْ (them) → "upon them." بِرَبِّهِمْ = بِـ (in/by) + رَبّ (Lord) + هِمْ (their) → "in their Lord." This density is why Quranic Arabic packs so much meaning into so few words — and why recognizing suffixes is essential for reading fluency.

Demonstrative Pronouns

أسماء الإشارة

Arabic demonstratives distinguish gender, number, AND proximity (near vs. far). In the Quran, ذٰلِكَ ("that," distant) often refers to God's Book or decree with a tone of reverence and grandeur, while هٰذا ("this," near) refers to present, tangible things.

هٰذَا
hādhā
This (m.)
Near demonstrative All Arabic
Points to something present or close. هٰذَا بَلَاغٌ لِلنَّاسِ — "This is a message for the people."
هٰذِهِ
hādhihi
This (f.)
Near demonstrative All Arabic
For feminine nouns. هٰذِهِ جَهَنَّمُ — "This is Hell."
ذٰلِكَ
dhālika
That (m.)
Far demonstrative All Arabic
Distance can be physical or reverential. The Quran's second verse uses it grandly:
ذَٰلِكَ الْكِتَابُ لَا رَيْبَ فِيهِ
"That is the Book — no doubt in it."
Al-Baqarah (2:2)
تِلْكَ
tilka
That (f.)
Far demonstrative All Arabic
For feminine distant reference. تِلْكَ الدَّارُ الْآخِرَةُ — "That is the home of the Hereafter."
هٰؤُلَاءِ
hāʾulāʾi
These (pl.)
Near demonstrative All Arabic
Plural demonstrative for both genders.
أُولٰئِكَ
ulāʾika
Those (pl.)
Far demonstrative All Arabic
One of the most common words in the Quran. أُولٰئِكَ عَلَى هُدًى مِّن رَّبِّهِمْ — "Those are upon guidance from their Lord."

Relative Pronouns

الأسماء الموصولة

Relative pronouns connect a noun to a descriptive clause: "those who believe," "the book which was sent down." Arabic relative pronouns agree in gender and number with the noun they refer to — so الَّذِي is for masculine singular, الَّتِي for feminine singular, and الَّذِينَ for masculine plural. This is the "Ladheena" you encounter on almost every page of the Quran.

الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا وَعَمِلُوا الصَّالِحَاتِ
alladhīna āmanū wa-ʿamilū ṣ-ṣāliḥāt
"Those who believed and did righteous deeds"
This phrase appears over 60 times in the Quran — it's the single most repeated description of the believers. الَّذِينَ (alladhīna) is the masculine plural relative pronoun "those who / who." Whenever the Quran describes a group, this is the word that introduces them.
الَّذِي
alladhī
Who / which / that (m. sg.)
Relative pronoun All Arabic
Masculine singular. الحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ الَّذِي خَلَقَ السَّمَاوَاتِ — "Praise to God who created the heavens."
الَّتِي
allatī
Who / which / that (f. sg.)
Relative pronoun All Arabic
Feminine singular. الجَنَّةَ الَّتِي وُعِدَ المُتَّقُونَ — "The Garden which the righteous were promised."
الَّذِينَ
alladhīna
Those who / who (m. pl.)
Relative pronoun All Arabic
The most frequent relative pronoun in the Quran. Appears in hundreds of verses. Introduces believers, disbelievers, and every group in between.
صِرَاطَ الَّذِينَ أَنْعَمْتَ عَلَيْهِمْ
"The path of those You have blessed"
Al-Fātiḥah (1:7)

Masculine dual — for exactly two male referents. Rare even in written MSA; the dual relative is one of the least-used forms in modern Arabic.
اللَّتَانِ
allatāni
Who / which (f. dual)
Relative pronoun Classical & Formal MSA
Feminine dual — for exactly two female referents. Like اللَّذانِ, this form is largely confined to formal written Arabic and classical texts.
مَا

Used for non-human / abstract referents. وَلَا تَقْفُ مَا لَيْسَ لَكَ بِهِ عِلْمٌ — "And do not pursue that of which you have no knowledge." Also doubles as a question word ("what?") and a negation particle.

Negation Particles

أدوات النفي

Arabic has a rich negation system where each particle operates in a specific tense and grammatical environment. This is one area where Arabic is more precise than English — instead of one "not," there are distinct words for negating past, present, future, and existential statements.

لَا
lā
No / not
General negation All Arabic
The Swiss Army knife of negation. Negates present/future verbs, or functions as an absolute negation (لَا إِلٰهَ إِلَّا اللهُ — "there is no god but God").
لَا إِكْرَاهَ فِي الدِّينِ
"There is no compulsion in religion."
Al-Baqarah (2:256)
لَمْ
lam
Did not (past negation)
Past negation Classical & Formal MSA
Negates the past tense but takes a present-tense (jussive) verb. لَمْ يَلِدْ وَلَمْ يُولَدْ — "He neither begot nor was begotten." This is the standard past-tense negation in the Quran.
لَنْ
lan
Will not (emphatic future negation)
Future negation Classical & Formal MSA
Negates the future emphatically. Takes a subjunctive verb. لَنْ تَنَالُوا الْبِرَّ حَتَّىٰ تُنفِقُوا — "You will never attain righteousness until you spend."
لَيْسَ
laysa
Is not / are not
Existential negation All Arabic
Negates identity or existence in the present. Grammatically a verb but functions like "is not." لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِ شَيْءٌ — "There is nothing like Him."
مَا
mā
Not (present/past negation)
Negation particle All Arabic
Versatile negator. Negates past verbs: مَا كَذَبَ — "he did not lie." Also negates nominal sentences in Ḥijāzī dialect (used in Quran): مَا هٰذَا بَشَرًا — "this is not a human."

💡 Tense encoded in the negation word

English uses "not" for all tenses and relies on the verb to show when: "did not go," "will not go," "does not go." Arabic encodes the tense in the negation particle itself:

مَا ذَهَبَ — "he did not go" (past, using past verb)
لَمْ يَذْهَبْ — "he did not go" (past, using jussive present verb — more formal)
لَا يَذْهَبُ — "he does not go" (present)
لَنْ يَذْهَبَ — "he will not go" (future, emphatic)

Once you internalize which particle goes with which tense, negation in Arabic becomes more precise than in English.

Emphasis & Assertion Particles

أدوات التوكيد

These particles add layers of emphasis, certainty, or contrast. The Quran uses them extensively to mark statements of absolute truth, divine promises, and solemn oaths. Recognizing them helps you feel the weight of a verse — whether it's a gentle reminder or an emphatic declaration.

إِنَّ
inna
Indeed / verily
Emphatic particle Classical & Formal MSA
The queen of Arabic emphasis. Opens assertions of fact. One of the "sisters of إنَّ" — a group of particles that take a noun in accusative + a predicate. Appears in virtually every page of the Quran.
قَدْ
qad
Indeed (past) / may (present)
Emphasis / possibility Classical & Formal MSA
With past verbs: certainty — قَدْ أَفْلَحَ ("he has certainly succeeded"). With present verbs: possibility — قَدْ يَعْلَمُ ("He may well know"). Context determines which.
قَدْ أَفْلَحَ الْمُؤْمِنُونَ
"Certainly the believers have succeeded."
Al-Muʾminūn (23:1)
لَقَدْ
laqad
Indeed / certainly (emphatic past)
Double emphasis Classical & Formal MSA
لَـ (emphatic lām) + قَدْ = double emphasis. The strongest way to assert that something happened. لَقَدْ خَلَقْنَا الْإِنسَانَ — "We have certainly created mankind."
أَلَا
alā
Verily! / Unquestionably!
Attention particle Quranic / Classical
A rhetorical attention-grabber. Essentially confined to the Quran and classical poetry — you would never hear this in modern conversation or even most formal writing.. Calls the listener to pay close attention to an incontestable fact.
أَلَا إِنَّ أَوْلِيَاءَ اللَّهِ لَا خَوْفٌ عَلَيْهِمْ
"Unquestionably, the allies of God — no fear will there be on them."
Yūnus (10:62)

Conditional & Temporal Particles

أدوات الشرط والزمان

Arabic conditional particles specify whether a condition is likely, unlikely, or purely hypothetical — a distinction English makes clumsily through verb tense ("if he goes" vs. "if he were to go") but Arabic makes explicitly through the choice of particle.

إِذَا
idhā
When / if (likely)
Conditional — likely All Arabic
For conditions expected to happen. إِذَا جَاءَ نَصْرُ اللهِ — "When the victory of God comes" (not "if" — it will come). Takes past-tense verb for future meaning.
إِذَا جَاءَ نَصْرُ اللَّهِ وَالْفَتْحُ
"When the victory of God has come and the conquest..."
An-Naṣr (110:1)
إِنْ
in
If (uncertain)
Conditional — uncertain All Arabic
For uncertain conditions — the outcome is unknown. وَإِنْ تَعُدُّوا نِعْمَةَ اللهِ لَا تُحْصُوهَا — "And if you should count the favors of God, you could not enumerate them."
لَوْ
law
If (hypothetical / contrary to fact)
Conditional — counterfactual All Arabic
For conditions that did NOT happen (counterfactual). لَوْ كُنتُ أَعْلَمُ الْغَيْبَ — "If I had known the unseen..." (but I don't).
لَوْلَا
lawlā
If not for / were it not for
Conditional — but-for Classical & Formal MSA
Counterfactual with a preventing element. لَوْلَا أَنتُمْ لَكُنَّا مُؤْمِنِينَ — "If not for you, we would have been believers."
حَيْثُ
ḥaythu
Where / wherever
Locative Classical & Formal MSA
Indicates location. وَحَيْثُ مَا كُنتُمْ فَوَلُّوا وُجُوهَكُمْ شَطْرَهُ — "Wherever you are, turn your faces toward it."
كَيْ / لِكَيْ
kay / li-kay
In order to / so that
Purpose Classical & Formal MSA
Introduces purpose clauses. كَيْ لَا يَكُونَ دُولَةً — "so that it does not become a perpetual distribution." Takes subjunctive verb.

💡 The إذا / إنْ / لَوْ spectrum

These three conditionals form a probability spectrum that the Quran uses with precision:

إِذَا — "When" (expected to happen). Used for resurrection, Day of Judgment, inevitable events.
إِنْ — "If" (genuinely uncertain). Used for human choices, moral tests.
لَوْ — "If" (didn't/won't happen). Used for counterfactuals, impossible scenarios.

When the Quran says إِذَا السَّمَاءُ انشَقَّتْ ("When the sky splits open"), the choice of إِذَا tells you: this will happen. It's not a question of "if."
